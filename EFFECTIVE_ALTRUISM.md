# Effective Altruism: Tenets and Origins

Effective altruism (EA) is a philosophical and social movement that applies
evidence and reason to the question of how to do the most good with one's
resources. This document summarizes its core ideas and intellectual roots.

## Basic Tenets

### 1. Impartiality / equal consideration of interests
Everyone's wellbeing counts equally, regardless of nationality, species, or
distance from us. A life saved in a distant country matters as much as one
saved next door.

### 2. Cause prioritization
Not all ways of doing good are equal — some interventions are hundreds or
thousands of times more effective than others. EA emphasizes choosing
*between* causes, not just executing well within one. Causes are often
evaluated on three dimensions:

- **Scale** — how big is the problem?
- **Tractability** — how solvable is it?
- **Neglectedness** — how many resources already go to it?

### 3. Evidence and reason over intuition
Prioritize interventions with strong empirical backing (e.g., randomized
controlled trials) and expected-value reasoning, rather than emotional appeal
or good intentions alone.

### 4. Cost-effectiveness
Maximize good done per dollar or hour. Charities can differ enormously in
effectiveness, so measurement matters.

### 5. Counterfactual thinking
Ask what would happen *anyway* without your contribution. The relevant
question is the marginal difference you make.

### 6. Cause-neutrality and openness
Start from "how can I do the most good?" rather than committing to a
pre-chosen cause, and update based on evidence.

## Common Focus Areas

- **Global health and development** — e.g., malaria nets, deworming, direct
  cash transfers
- **Animal welfare** — especially factory farming
- **Longtermism / existential risk** — pandemics, AI safety, nuclear risk;
  the view that positively influencing the long-term future is a key moral
  priority

## Origins and Original Works

### Philosophical foundations
- **Peter Singer**, *"Famine, Affluence, and Morality"* (1972) — the
  foundational essay. Singer's drowning-child thought experiment argues that
  if we can prevent great suffering at little cost, we're morally obligated to
  do so, and physical distance is morally irrelevant. His later book
  *The Life You Can Save* (2009) popularized these ideas.
- Broader roots in **utilitarianism** — Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill
  (18th–19th c.), and the "impartial spectator" tradition.

### Formation of the modern movement (late 2000s–2010s)
- **GiveWell** (2007), founded by Holden Karnofsky and Elie Hassenfeld —
  rigorous charity evaluation focused on cost-effectiveness.
- **Giving What We Can** (2009), founded by philosopher **Toby Ord** (with
  William MacAskill) at Oxford — a pledge to donate a significant portion of
  income (typically 10%) to effective charities.
- **80,000 Hours** (2011), co-founded by **William MacAskill** and
  **Benjamin Todd** — advice on high-impact careers.
- The term "effective altruism" was coined around 2011, and the **Centre for
  Effective Altruism** was established at Oxford.

### Key books
- **William MacAskill**, *Doing Good Better* (2015) — the movement's most
  accessible manifesto.
- **Peter Singer**, *The Most Good You Can Do* (2015).
- **Toby Ord**, *The Precipice* (2020) — the case for taking existential risk
  seriously (longtermism).
- **William MacAskill**, *What We Owe the Future* (2022) — the major popular
  statement of longtermism.

### Related concepts
- **Earning to give** — pursuing a high-income career to donate more
  (associated with early 80,000 Hours advice).
- **Longtermism** — the more recent and philosophically contested branch.

## A Note on Criticism

EA has faced substantive critiques worth knowing:

- Over-reliance on quantification (measurability bias)
- The philosophical controversy around longtermism prioritizing hypothetical
  future people
- Questions about elite / technocratic framing
- Reputational fallout from the 2022 collapse of FTX, whose founder Sam
  Bankman-Fried was a prominent EA funder

These debates are active within and outside the movement.
