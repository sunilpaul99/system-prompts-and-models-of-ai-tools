# Stress Test: Content Economy Briefing vs. Current Sources

*Wiki entry. Verification of load-bearing claims in `raw/content-economy-briefing.md` (dated April 2026) against live sources, checked July 18, 2026.*

## Confirmed

- **Tow Center/CJR citation study**: Real ([Nieman Lab](https://www.niemanlab.org/2025/03/ai-search-engines-fail-to-produce-accurate-citations-in-over-60-of-tests-according-to-new-tow-center-study/), March 2025). Eight AI search tools, 60%+ incorrect overall; Grok 3 wrong 94% of the time, Perplexity best at 37%. **Nuance the briefing omits**: the test was a specific hard task — identify the source/date/URL of a quoted excerpt — not general citation accuracy. "60% of all AI citations are wrong" overstates the finding; "AI tools fail source-identification 60% of the time and are confidently wrong" is accurate.
- **Microsoft PCM**: Real and as described. [Feb 2026 announcement](https://about.ads.microsoft.com/en/blog/post/february-2026/building-toward-a-sustainable-content-economy-for-the-agentic-web), Nikhil Kolar [Digiday Q&A](https://digiday.com/media/qa-nikhil-kolar-vp-microsoft-ai-scales-its-click-to-sign-ai-content-marketplace/), partner list matches (AP, Business Insider, Condé Nast, Hearst, People, USA TODAY, Vox). Still pilot-stage; standardized contract not finalized; **no payout data — unchanged**.
- **NYT v. OpenAI/Microsoft posture**: Discovery war as described. No settlement as of July 2026.
- **Karpathy AutoResearch**: Real ([github.com/karpathy/autoresearch](https://github.com/karpathy/autoresearch), March 2026); 700 experiments/2 days figure checks out; star count in briefing slightly inflated vs. ~50K reported at 3 weeks.
- **NYT–Amazon deal** ($20–25M/yr): corroborated in current reporting ([Quartz](https://qz.com/ai-training-data-pricing-licensing-deals-market-052126)).

## Could not confirm — treat as directional, single-source

- **"99.6% of AI usage is invisible" (Otterly.AI)**: Source blog inaccessible; the number appears nowhere else independently. It is a single vendor's sample — and Otterly sells AI-visibility monitoring, so it has an interest in the scariest framing. The *direction* (usage vastly exceeds citation) is almost certainly right; the precise figure should not be quoted as established.
- **Aggregate licensing totals ($2.92B commitments / ~$817M/yr / ~$24M avg deal)**: No independent corroboration found; different trackers use incompatible definitions (training vs. grounding, commitments vs. annual). Use "roughly $1–3B in total commitments, low hundreds of millions to ~$1B/yr" and cite the specific deals instead (News Corp $250M/5yr, FT $5–10M/yr, Wiley $49M in FY2026).
- **Demand-side percentages** (64% of Gen Z use TikTok as search engine, etc.): vendor surveys (Adobe, Sprout Social) — directionally consistent across sources, precision is marketing-grade.
- **Scenario probabilities (Ch. 16)**: unfalsifiable vibes; use the scenarios, drop the percentages.

## Known bias

Prepared from a Microsoft-adjacent vantage point: PCM receives the most detailed and sympathetic treatment; "Microsoft is the publisher-friendly one" rests largely on one Digiday scorecard. History and technical chapters look solid; competitive framing deserves independent skepticism.

## Developments since April 2026 (the briefing is already stale here)

1. **NYT case escalated rather than settling.** July 9, 2026: NYT-led plaintiffs moved to **sanction OpenAI**, alleging it falsely told the court it couldn't search its models for plaintiffs' works while having done exactly that before the suit was filed ([US News/Reuters](https://money.usnews.com/investing/news/articles/2026-07-09/new-york-times-led-group-asks-court-to-sanction-openai-in-us-copyright-dispute), [The Hill](https://thehill.com/policy/technology/5961769-newspapers-sue-openai-evidence/)). Track: summary judgment briefing 2026, possible trial 2027 ([Mesh IP tracker](https://www.meshiplaw.com/litigation-tracker/new-york-times-microsoft)). The briefing's "likely settles in 2026" reads less likely now; analyst settlement chatter has moved to $2–5B.
2. **Google entered the licensing market.** Negotiating with ~20 national publishers ([Press Gazette](https://pressgazette.co.uk/platforms/google-announces-first-ai-deals-with-publishers/), [MediaPost](https://www.mediapost.com/publications/article/416113/google-looks-to-reportedly-broaden-publisher-licen.html)); "AI-powered article overviews" pilot with Washington Post and The Guardian; AP real-time deal for Gemini; Reddit at $60M/yr. The briefing's frame of Google-as-holdout (the Nieman "market price is zero" argument) is now partially outdated — though reporting notes Google is taking a tough line on terms.
3. **Regulatory leverage materialized.** June 3, 2026: UK CMA imposed conduct requirements on Google including publisher opt-out controls for AI search features — the first live enforcement of the "duty to accommodate publishers" kind, and the proximate driver of Google's dealmaking. Next fixed date: **EU AI Act transparency rules, August 2, 2026**.

## Net assessment

The briefing's architecture holds: the harm data, the attribution analysis, and the legal map are sound. Its two weakest habits are (a) quoting single-vendor precision as fact, and (b) an April-2026 freeze that now understates both the legal escalation and Google's entry. Directionally, events since April moved toward the briefing's Scenario A/B boundary: more regulatory compulsion, more licensing, no settlement.
