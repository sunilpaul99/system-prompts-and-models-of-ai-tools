# Attribution Deep Dive: Why Paying Publishers Fairly Is Technically Hard

*Wiki entry. Source material: `raw/content-economy-briefing.md` (Ch. 6–9), plus independent analysis. Last updated July 2026.*

## 1. Three different problems that get conflated as "attribution"

Any discussion of AI attribution goes wrong immediately unless these are separated:

1. **Provenance** — where did this content come from, and is it authentic? (C2PA, watermarking, signed manifests.) Solved-ish for images, barely started for text.
2. **Citation** — which sources does the AI *say* it used? Generated by the LLM itself as a text-generation task. Unreliable: the Tow Center found 60%+ error rates on a source-identification task across eight products.
3. **Value attribution** — which sources actually *shaped* the answer, and by how much? This is the one money depends on. **No production system measures it.**

The industry ships (1) and (2) and markets them as (3).

## 2. The correct answer and why it's unaffordable

The provably fair split of a cooperative output is the **Shapley value**: each source's average marginal contribution across every possible subset of sources. It is the unique allocation satisfying efficiency (shares sum to the whole), symmetry (equal contributors get equal shares), dummy (unused sources get zero), and additivity.

The cost: n sources require 2^n model evaluations. At 15 retrieved passages that's 32,768 forward passes — roughly **$320 per query** against a query that costs ~$0.01 to serve and earns maybe a few cents. Monte Carlo sampling gets it to ~$0.45/query — still 10–50x the value of the query itself. The attribution budget for a real product is a fraction of a cent, which is why everything actually deployed is a heuristic.

## 3. The measurement ladder

From cheapest to most faithful, with the failure mode of each:

| Method | Cost | Failure mode |
|---|---|---|
| Retrieval logs ("it was in the context window") | ~0 | Retrieved ≠ used. Pays sources the model ignored. |
| LLM self-citation | ~0 | Probabilistic text generation; hallucinated, missing, and wrong citations. |
| Embedding similarity (output vs. each source) | ~0 | Similarity ≠ causation; rewards paraphrase-adjacent content. |
| Claim decomposition + matching (ProRata's "Gist") | low | Black box, unaudited; matching ≠ influence. |
| Counterfactual ablation / leave-one-out (RAGonite) | n+1 passes (~2x query cost) | **The redundancy trap — see §4.** |
| Monte Carlo Shapley | ~45x query cost | Approximation error; still uneconomic. |
| Exact Shapley | 2^n passes | Intractable. |
| Training-data influence (TRAK, TrackStar) | offline, enormous | 8B-param scale at best; not per-query; training-time only. |

The practical frontier is counterfactual ablation: remove each source, regenerate, measure how much the answer changes. One extra forward pass per source is affordable for high-value queries.

## 4. The redundancy trap (the most important subtlety)

Leave-one-out has a fatal flaw that Shapley was designed to fix: **if two sources contain the same fact, removing either one changes nothing** — so both measure as worthless, even though the fact came entirely from them. Shapley splits the credit between them instead of zeroing both.

This is not an edge case; it is the normal case for news. Most journalism is redundant with other journalism within hours. Wire copy, rewrites, and aggregation mean that for a typical query, 10 retrieved sources contain 2–3 distinct pieces of information.

Two consequences:

1. Any cheap attribution scheme either over-pays redundant sources (retrieval-based) or zeroes them all (leave-one-out). Getting redundancy right is exactly the expensive part of Shapley. Cluster Shapley (group similar documents, compute Shapley over clusters) is the published attempt to shortcut this.
2. **Fair attribution is not publisher-friendly.** Under genuinely fair value attribution, commodity coverage — the vast middle of competent, duplicative reporting — earns approximately nothing, because its marginal information content is approximately zero. Uniqueness is the asset: scoops, original data, exclusive access, distinctive analysis. And a scoop's uniqueness decays in minutes once others rewrite it, unless the licensing regime pays the originator rather than the fastest paraphraser. "Fairness" concentrates revenue even harder than the Spotify model does.

## 5. Attribution is an incentive design problem, not just a measurement problem

Whatever metric gets paid becomes the thing publishers optimize (Goodhart's law):

- Pay per **retrieval** → GEO/AEO spam: engineer content to get pulled into context windows. (This industry already exists.)
- Pay per **citation** → optimize for being citable — quotable phrasing, authority signals — regardless of substance.
- Pay per **counterfactual influence** → produce genuinely non-redundant information. This is the only metric whose gaming behavior is socially useful — gaming it *is* doing journalism.

This gives a first-principles argument for influence-based attribution that doesn't depend on fairness rhetoric: it's the only payable metric aligned with the production of new information.

## 6. The training/grounding blur

Grounding deals (per-use, recurring — the PCM model) assume the retrieved content is what produced the answer. But if the model was also *trained* on the publisher's archive, part of every answer draws on unpaid parametric knowledge, and no method can apportion between the two. TrackStar's key finding cuts here: the training data that *causally shaped* model behavior is not the same as the data that *contains the facts* — keyword search finds the latter, influence tracing finds the former, and they diverge. Every grounding-only deal implicitly prices training contribution at zero.

## 7. Where the research is converging

Threads that exist separately (briefing Ch. 9): citation-prediction from embeddings (SPECTER2, EDM), embedding-uniqueness-predicts-impact (Shibayama, Kojaku), data valuation (Data Shapley, TRAK/TrackStar), counterfactual RAG (RAGonite), and shipping heuristics (ProRata, Bria's image-domain royalty system — the closest commercial analog).

The identified white space: **no one has used citation-validated uniqueness in embedding space as a trained proxy for content value, then deployed it for attribution.** The claimed patent space is open. Caveats from the source literature itself: within-document text novelty does *not* predict citation impact (the signal is in combinations of references, not surface novelty); academic citation is a specific social practice that may not transfer to news.

A realistic production stack for the next 2–3 years: counterfactual ablation for per-query credit on high-value queries + corpus-level uniqueness scoring for base rates + policy rules for the split — presented honestly as policy, not measurement.

## 8. Bottom line

- Attribution at production scale is a **policy decision dressed as measurement**, and will remain so for years.
- The real fight is therefore over **who sets the split rules and whether they're auditable** — not over algorithms.
- Truly fair attribution, if it arrives, **helps unique content and destroys commodity content**. Publishers lobbying for "fair attribution" should understand they are lobbying for a uniqueness-weighted payout curve.
- The metric chosen will reshape journalism itself, because it will be optimized against.
