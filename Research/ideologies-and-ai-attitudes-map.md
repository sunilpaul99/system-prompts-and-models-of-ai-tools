## 1. Introduction: Purpose and Method

This document maps the world's major ideologies and belief groups onto their views of artificial intelligence. It is written for a reader who wants to *influence* the opinions of these groups — which means each entry must answer five questions: What does this group believe at its core? What are its canonical texts? How do those beliefs translate into attitudes toward AI (existential risk, jobs, human dignity, openness, regulation)? Who are the gatekeepers who move opinion inside the group? And what persuasion levers work, given the group's own moral vocabulary?

Two structural facts organize everything that follows.

**First, the people who build AI and the people who will live with it belong to almost entirely disjoint belief communities.** The ideologies that dominate AI research and industry — effective altruism, rationalism, effective accelerationism, transhumanism, the critical AI-ethics school — together count perhaps a few hundred thousand adherents worldwide, yet they have set the terms of the entire AI debate: the words "alignment," "x-risk," "AI safety," "responsible AI," and "acceleration" all come from these micro-ideologies. Meanwhile the world's large belief groups — 2.4 billion Christians, 1.9 billion Muslims, 1.2 billion Hindus, half a billion Buddhists, and the great political blocs — are only now developing formal positions on AI, and they are doing so in their own moral vocabularies, which the AI world largely does not speak. The influence opportunity lies precisely in this translation gap.

**Second, attitudes toward AI are not yet politically "frozen."** Unlike climate change or vaccination, AI has not yet been fully absorbed into existing partisan or sectarian identity structures. Survey data (Section 6) show AI anxiety crossing party, class, and confessional lines in unusual ways — American conservatives and progressive AI-ethics scholars both distrust Big Tech; Catholic bishops and secular labor unions both center human dignity and work. Coalitions are still available across normally hostile borders. Every case study in Section 7 (SB-1047, the EU AI Act, the open-weights fight, the OpenAI board crisis) shows opinion moving quickly when the right messenger used the right frame. This window will close as AI politics polarizes; the groups mapped here are still persuadable.

**Method.** This synthesis was produced through a multi-agent deep-research process: parallel search sweeps across (a) belief-evaluation frameworks, (b) the elite ideologies of the AI industry, (c) large political ideologies, (d) religious traditions (a dedicated five-track deep dive), and (e) empirical survey data and documented cases of opinion change — followed by adversarial verification of claims and citations. A verification caveat applies throughout: the research environment permitted web *search* corroboration across multiple independent sources but blocked direct fetching of most primary documents; citations below were each corroborated by two or more independent sources, and known misattributions discovered during verification are flagged inline. Citations to pre-2023 canonical works (Bostrom, Singer, Haidt, Douglas & Wildavsky, etc.) are standard published editions.

## 2. Frameworks for Evaluating Beliefs

No single lens organizes "belief groups and AI" adequately. Five frameworks are evaluated here; the practical recommendation is to use them in layers — Moral Foundations to craft the message, Cultural Theory to predict the risk reflex, Inglehart–Welzel to locate the society, the AI-tribes taxonomy to navigate the industry, and TESCREAL only to understand one influential critique.

### 2.1 Moral Foundations Theory (MFT)

**Original works:** Jonathan Haidt and Jesse Graham, "When Morality Opposes Justice: Conservatives Have Moral Intuitions that Liberals May Not Recognize," *Social Justice Research* 20 (2007); Jesse Graham, Jonathan Haidt, and Brian Nosek, "Liberals and Conservatives Rely on Different Sets of Moral Foundations," *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology* 96:5 (2009); Jonathan Haidt, *The Righteous Mind: Why Good People Are Divided by Politics and Religion* (Pantheon, 2012).

MFT holds that moral judgment rests on a small set of intuitive foundations — care/harm, fairness/cheating, loyalty/betrayal, authority/subversion, sanctity/degradation (later liberty/oppression) — and that political liberals moralize mainly the first two while conservatives moralize all of them. Its value for AI persuasion is direct: the same AI policy can be argued as *harm prevention* (progressives), as *protection of the sacred human person* (religious conservatives — note how precisely the Vatican's and the Southern Baptists' documents in Section 5 track the sanctity foundation), as *loyalty to nation and workers* (populists), or as *liberty against concentrated power* (libertarians — and note that anti-Big-Tech liberty framing is currently the only frame that reaches all four quadrants at once). MFT's weakness: it describes moral *style*, not doctrinal content; it cannot tell you what a Muslim jurist or a rationalist forecaster actually believes about machine agency. Use it to tune rhetoric, not to predict positions.

### 2.2 Cultural Theory of Risk and Cultural Cognition

**Original works:** Mary Douglas and Aaron Wildavsky, *Risk and Culture: An Essay on the Selection of Technological and Environmental Dangers* (University of California Press, 1982); Dan M. Kahan et al., "Culture and Identity-Protective Cognition: Explaining the White-Male Effect in Risk Perception," *Journal of Empirical Legal Studies* 4:3 (2007); Dan M. Kahan, "Cultural Cognition as a Conception of the Cultural Theory of Risk," in *Handbook of Risk Theory* (Springer, 2012).

Douglas and Wildavsky's central claim — that societies *select* which dangers to fear according to their form of social organization (hierarchical, individualist, egalitarian, fatalist) — is the single best predictor of how a group will process AI risk claims. Egalitarians (the AI-ethics school, the labor left) fear technologies that entrench corporate power and inequality; individualists (techno-libertarians, e/acc) discount technological risk and fear regulation itself; hierarchists (the Vatican, the CCP, national-security conservatives) fear disorder and loss of authoritative control — which is why both the Holy See and the Chinese state, from opposite metaphysics, converge on "human/state control over AI." Kahan's cultural-cognition research adds the operational lesson: people accept risk information from messengers who share their values and reject the same information from outsiders — *the messenger is the message*. This is the deepest framework for the influence use-case, and it explains the empirical strangeness documented in Sections 6–7 (e.g., evangelical wariness of AI aligning with secular AI-safety doomerism against libertarian optimism).

### 2.3 Inglehart–Welzel Values Dimensions

**Original works:** Ronald Inglehart, *The Silent Revolution: Changing Values and Political Styles Among Western Publics* (Princeton, 1977); Ronald Inglehart and Christian Welzel, *Modernization, Cultural Change, and Democracy: The Human Development Sequence* (Cambridge, 2005); the World Values Survey cultural map (worldvaluessurvey.org).

The WVS maps societies on two axes: traditional vs. secular-rational values, and survival vs. self-expression values. Its predictive use for AI: societies high in survival values (much of the Global South, post-Soviet space) evaluate AI primarily through jobs, security, and national standing; societies high in self-expression values (Protestant Europe, Anglosphere) evaluate it through autonomy, privacy, and rights. Cross-national polling (Ipsos, *Global Views on A.I.*, annual since 2022) consistently finds emerging-market publics far more AI-optimistic than rich-country publics — a pattern Inglehart–Welzel predicts and that most Western AI discourse ignores. Weakness: nation-level resolution; useless for intra-society persuasion.

### 2.4 The AI-Tribes Taxonomy (Doomers, Accelerationists, Ethicists, Pragmatists)

**Representative original works:** for the "doomer" pole, Eliezer Yudkowsky, "Pausing AI Developments Isn't Enough. We Need to Shut it All Down," *TIME*, March 29, 2023; for the accelerationist pole, Marc Andreessen, "The Techno-Optimist Manifesto" (a16z.com, October 16, 2023); for the ethics pole, Emily M. Bender, Timnit Gebru, Angelina McMillan-Major, and Margaret Mitchell (as "Shmargaret Shmitchell"), "On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots: Can Language Models Be Too Big? 🦜," *FAccT '21* (ACM, 2021); for the pragmatist center, the corporate "responsible scaling" literature (e.g., Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy, 2023).

This journalistic taxonomy — it crystallized in 2023 coverage of the ChatGPT moment — is the working map *inside* the AI industry, and Section 3 is organized around a refined version of it. Its virtue is currency; its vice is that it flattens deep ideological genealogies (EA utilitarianism, LessWrong epistemics, Landian accelerationism, critical race theory and STS in the FAccT school) into Twitter factions, and it has near-zero resolution outside the industry. Use it for navigating AI-researcher and lab-leader opinion; do not project it onto publics.

### 2.5 TESCREAL

**Original work:** Timnit Gebru and Émile P. Torres, "The TESCREAL bundle: Eugenics and the promise of utopia through artificial general intelligence," *First Monday* 29:4 (April 2024).

Gebru and Torres bundle Transhumanism, Extropianism, Singularitarianism, Cosmism, Rationalism, Effective Altruism, and Longtermism into a single lineage ("TESCREAL") which they trace to twentieth-century eugenics, arguing that the AGI project itself is the bundle's utopian program. As analysis, the bundle is contested — it groups movements that fight each other bitterly (EA safetyism vs. e/acc), and critics note the genealogical argument does more guilt-by-association than mechanism-tracing. But as a *political fact* it matters enormously: TESCREAL is the organizing frame through which the academic left, much of tech journalism, and parts of the policy world now interpret the entire AI-safety enterprise. Anyone seeking to influence progressive, academic, or civil-society audiences must know this frame; anyone seeking to influence EA/rationalist audiences must know they experience it as a slur. It is a lens to be understood, not adopted.

## 5. Religious Traditions and AI

Religious traditions are the largest belief groups on earth, and between 2019 and 2026 nearly all of them moved from silence to formal position-taking on AI. Three patterns cut across every tradition and should govern any influence strategy:

1. **The dignity consensus.** Every tradition that has issued a formal document grounds it in some version of human uniqueness — imago Dei, b'tselem Elohim, khalifa, atman, buddha-nature — and every one draws the same line: AI as *tool* is acceptable, AI as *person, authority, or object of trust* is not. This makes "human dignity and human control" the one frame that works across all religious audiences simultaneously.
2. **The clergy–laity adoption gap.** Clergy everywhere are simultaneously more theologically cautious about AI than their flocks *and* faster adopters of it for instrumental work (sermon preparation, administration, translation). Influence campaigns should treat clergy as pragmatic users with doctrinal red lines, not as Luddites.
3. **The authenticity red line.** The most visceral AI issue inside every tradition is synthetic religious authority: deepfaked preachers, chatbot fatwas, AI sermons, robot rites. Transparency and anti-deception commitments buy more goodwill with religious audiences than any safety or economic argument.

### 5.1 Catholicism (~1.4 billion)

**Core beliefs and canonical texts.** Catholic engagement with AI is the most institutionally developed of any religion, and it runs through a single doctrinal spine: the human person, created in the image of God (imago Dei), possesses an embodied, relational, spiritual intelligence that no computation can instantiate. The key documents, in order: the **Rome Call for AI Ethics** (Pontifical Academy for Life, February 28, 2020), signed at launch by Archbishop Vincenzo Paglia with Microsoft's Brad Smith, IBM's John Kelly III, the FAO's Qu Dongyu, and Italian minister Paola Pisano — six principles (transparency, inclusion, responsibility, impartiality, reliability, security/privacy) under the coinage "algorethics"; Pope Francis's Message for the 57th World Day of Peace, *Artificial Intelligence and Peace* (December 8, 2023), which calls for a **binding international treaty on AI** and a ban on lethal autonomous weapons; Francis's address to the G7 at Borgo Egnazia (June 14, 2024) — the first papal address to a G7 — declaring AI a "cognitive-industrial revolution" and insisting "no machine should ever choose to take the life of a human being"; and above all ***Antiqua et Nova: Note on the Relationship Between Artificial Intelligence and Human Intelligence*** (Dicastery for the Doctrine of the Faith and Dicastery for Culture and Education, January 28, 2025), the most complete magisterial statement any religion has produced on AI. *Antiqua et Nova* rejects functionalist (Turing-test) definitions of intelligence, insists AI must "complement, not replace" human intelligence, warns that substitution amounts to idolatry, addresses education, labor, health, relationships, misinformation, and war — and, remarkably, adopts explicit *existential-risk* language about autonomous weapons ("the potential to act in ways that could threaten the survival of entire regions or even of humanity itself"). Behind these texts stand *Laudato Si'* (2015), whose critique of the "technocratic paradigm" (§§101–114) supplies the standing anti-Big-Tech lens, and *Dignitas Infinita* (2024), which supplies the dignity vocabulary.

The election of **Pope Leo XIV** (May 8, 2025) made AI the signature issue of a pontificate: he told the College of Cardinals on May 10, 2025 that he chose his name because Leo XIII's *Rerum Novarum* (1891) answered the first industrial revolution, and the Church must now answer "another industrial revolution and... developments in the field of artificial intelligence that pose new challenges for the defence of human dignity, justice and labour." His first encyclical, ***Magnifica Humanitas: On Safeguarding the Human Person in the Time of Artificial Intelligence*** (signed May 15, 2026, the 135th anniversary of *Rerum Novarum*), argues technology "is never neutral, because it takes on the characteristics of those who devise, finance, regulate, and use it." In February 2026 he instructed Roman clergy not to write homilies with AI: AI "will never be able to share faith."

**Gatekeepers.** The Pontifical Academy for Life (Paglia); Fr. **Paolo Benanti**, the Franciscan engineer-ethicist who coined "algorethics," directs the Vatican's RenAIssance Foundation, served on the UN AI Advisory Body, and chaired the Italian government's AI commission — the single most influential religious AI-ethics broker in the world; the two doctrinal dicasteries; the USCCB; and the Jesuit intellectual network (Georgetown, Santa Clara's Markkula Center, *America*, *La Civiltà Cattolica*).

**Fault lines.** Vatican techno-engagement (Rome Call partnership with Microsoft, IBM, Cisco; AI-powered liturgical translation in ~60 languages) versus traditionalist currents reading the same documents apocalyptically; labor-dignity concerns versus Catholic tech-sector enthusiasm.

**Persuasion levers.** Human dignity is the trump card; "meaningful human control" maps exactly onto Francis's G7 formula; solidarity and the common good against concentration of AI power; subsidiarity against centralized algorithmic governance; "algorethics" as the accepted in-house term. The Church has already accepted existential-risk framing for autonomous weapons — extending that frame to other AI risks is a short doctrinal step. Approaches framed as *partnership in shaping* AI (the Rome Call model) succeed; approaches implying AI could be a person, a moral agent, or a spiritual authority fail instantly.

### 5.2 Eastern Orthodoxy (~220 million)

Orthodoxy has no Vatican-style document machine and — because of the 2018 Moscow–Constantinople schism — no unified position, but two sharply different registers.

**Moscow.** Patriarch **Kirill** frames digital technology apocalyptically: in a January 2019 Rossiya-1 interview he warned that centralized data collection is "a harbinger of the coming of the Antichrist... a personality that will be at the head of the world wide web controlling the entire human race," and in a January 30, 2025 address to the State Duma he compared AI to nuclear energy and repeated the Antichrist warning while demanding strict state control. Simultaneously, the ROC-linked **World Russian People's Council** calls for an "Orthodox AI" trained on "traditional values," warning that Western models carry "alien values" — the clearest fusion anywhere of religious rhetoric with state tech-nationalism. (A frequently repeated claim that Kirill delivered these warnings at Davos is a misattribution.)

**Constantinople.** Ecumenical Patriarch **Bartholomew's** register is humanist engagement: "Life is not digital — real life is more than measurable reality"; AI requires "respect of individual dignity, safeguard of fundamental freedoms, and advancement of social equity." His Christmas 2024 encyclical contrasted AI's aspirations with the Incarnation, and in June 2026 he blessed the creation of the ITHOS Institute (ethics, theology, and technology) in Thessaloniki. The Greek Orthodox Archdiocese of America runs an AI-Theology Working Group, and its 2024 Clergy-Laity Congress held a panel titled "Artificial Intelligence, Personhood, and Theosis."

**Doctrine and levers.** The theological resources are **theosis** (deification — a destiny no artifact can share, hence no machine personhood), Metropolitan John Zizioulas's relational ontology of personhood (*Being as Communion*, 1985 — simulation is not communion), and icon theology (the human as living icon versus the simulacrum). For Greek-sphere audiences, dignity/freedom/equity language compatible with EU governance vocabulary works; for the Russian sphere, only sovereignty and traditional-values framing penetrates, and Western messengers are structurally distrusted (Kahan's rule at maximum strength).

### 5.3 Protestantism: Evangelical, Mainline, Pentecostal, and Latter-day Saints (~900 million combined)

**Evangelicals and Southern Baptists.** The anchor documents are the ERLC's **"Artificial Intelligence: An Evangelical Statement of Principles"** (April 2019; lead drafter **Jason Thacker**; ~70 signatories including then-ERLC president Russell Moore) — twelve articles of affirmations and denials built on imago Dei, denying that technology may "usurp or subvert the dominion and stewardship... entrusted solely to humanity by God" or be assigned "human identity, worth, dignity, or moral agency" — and the Southern Baptist Convention's resolution **"On Artificial Intelligence and Emerging Technologies"** (June 13, 2023, New Orleans), the first denominational AI resolution anywhere, which grounds dignity "in being image bearers — not in what we do" and pointedly calls for engagement "from a place of eschatological hope rather than uncritical embrace or fearful rejection." Thacker's *The Age of AI* (Zondervan, 2020) is the standard book-length treatment. The internal spectrum runs from a well-funded "redemptive technology" wing (the Gloo platform's "AI for Human Flourishing"; FaithTech; Bible-translation AI as consensus killer app) to skeptics for whom the 2025 episode of fake AI-generated John MacArthur sermons on YouTube is the standing exhibit. Popular evangelical discourse also carries a live end-times register (Greg Laurie's "Is AI the Antichrist?" — answer: no, but it could build Revelation 13's deception infrastructure) that denominational elites deliberately dampen. Adoption is racing ahead of comfort: by 2025–26 roughly two-thirds of pastors used AI in sermon preparation while ~43% of churchgoers objected (Barna/Gloo; Lifeway Research, April 2026). **Levers:** lead with imago Dei; frame AI as "tool, not idol"; offer Great Commission utility (translation, evangelism reach); make transparency/anti-deepfake commitments concrete. **Never** suggest AI could be spiritual, sentient, or trustworthy as an authority — that trips the idolatry and end-times alarms at once.

**Mainline Protestants.** The Church of England's General Synod (February 26, 2024) affirmed work as key to "human dignity and purpose" facing the AI "industrial revolution"; Archbishop Justin Welby signed the Rome Call on April 30, 2024 ("the dignity of every human being, created by God, not for profit or productivity"). The ELCA issued an "Artificial Intelligence Issue Paper" (December 2025, on the premise that technology is "not socially neutral"); the PCUSA's General Assembly directed creation of an AI social-witness policy; Episcopal, Lutheran, and Presbyterian bodies convened a joint "AI & The Church Summit." **Levers:** justice vocabulary — labor displacement, algorithmic bias, creation care (data-center energy) — delivered through study-commission process, not evangelistic utility.

**Pentecostal/charismatic Christianity (the fastest-growing Christian family, ~600 million).** The headline finding is an *absence*: no verifiable formal AI statements exist from the prosperity-gospel and charismatic megachurch world (Copeland, Osteen, Bethel, Hillsong). The discourse instead lives in two places: prophecy media (Jonathan Cahn and the Charisma ecosystem treating AI as end-times *deception infrastructure* — the False Prophet's toolkit, not the beast itself) and pragmatic Global-South adoption — Nigeria's Redeemed Christian Church of God (~9 million members) runs an AI chatbot ("Apostle Stephen"), and Deeper Life's "Ask Kumuyi" answers questions from the pastor's sermon corpus. **Levers:** anointing and discernment vocabulary ("the Spirit, not the algorithm"), deepfake anxiety mapped onto "lying wonders" (2 Thess. 2:9), and harvest/amplification framing. Secular-regulatory language does not land; spiritual-warfare and harvest language does.

**Latter-day Saints (~17 million, disproportionately influential in US tech).** The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints published **"Guiding Principles for the Church's Use of Artificial Intelligence"** (March 13, 2024, introduced by Elder Gerrit W. Gong and Elder John C. Pingree): AI must "support and not supplant connection between God and His children," with transparency and attribution requirements. Elder David A. Bednar's worldwide devotional "Things as They Really Are 2.0" (November 3, 2024) warned AI can "diminish and suffocate your moral agency" — humans can "think celestial" while AI "only has the ability to think telestial." LDS theology is structurally resistant to uploading-style transhumanism (embodiment is essential to exaltation, D&C 93) while institutionally pragmatic (FamilySearch runs one of religion's largest AI deployments; BYU leads a multi-faith research consortium that found major AI models systematically omit religious perspectives). **Levers:** quote their own "support, not supplant" principle; frame design choices as protecting moral agency; family-history AI is the unambiguous good; deepfake protection of church leaders is a strong hook.

### 5.4 Islam (~1.9 billion)

**Core beliefs and canonical rulings.** Islamic engagement runs through jurisprudence rather than theology-of-mind: the default legal ruling on new technology is permissibility (*ibaha*), assessed by use rather than essence, against the objectives of the law (*maqasid al-sharia*: protection of religion, life, intellect, lineage, property). The foundational academic text is Amana Raquib, *Islamic Ethics of Technology: An Objectives (Maqasid) Approach* (Islamic Book Trust, 2015). Because only God breathes spirit (*ruh*, Qur'an 15:29), AI is universally treated as a soulless artifact incapable of intention (*niyyah*) or moral responsibility (*taklif*) — hence it can never be a mufti or lead prayer. The landmark transnational ruling is the OIC's International Islamic Fiqh Academy **Resolution No. 258 (3/26), "Artificial Intelligence: Its Rulings, Guidelines, and Ethics"** (26th session, Doha, May 4–8, 2025): AI development and use are permissible subject to conditions (lawful purpose, benefit and harm-prevention, no abuse of religions, data protection, security, transparency), and AI cannot replace the human jurist. Other key acts: Sheikh **Abdallah bin Bayyah** (chairman, UAE Fatwa Council) signed the Rome Call's Abrahamic commitment at the Vatican on January 10, 2023 (note: Grand Imam Ahmed el-Tayeb of Al-Azhar did *not* sign — a common misattribution); Indonesia's **Nahdlatul Ulama** — the world's largest Muslim organization — ruled at its September 2023 Munas that asking AI religious questions is permissible but *haram* to act on AI answers as fatwas, and recommended building its own AI on its own textual corpus; Egypt's **Dar al-Ifta** (January 2026) banned AI Qur'an interpretation as mere "statistical processing without comprehension"; and Dubai's pioneering "Virtual Ifta" AI fatwa service (2019) was quietly discontinued within two years.

**Gatekeepers.** Al-Azhar (prestige and ethics diplomacy; el-Tayeb warns unregulated AI puts "control over humanity's future in the hands of a few"); Egypt's Dar al-Ifta (most active fatwa producer); bin Bayyah's UAE/Abu Dhabi network (interfaith AI diplomacy); the IIFA (transnational collective ijtihad); Indonesia's NU and Muhammadiyah; Turkey's Diyanet (cautious instrumental adoption, no fatwa); Shia marjas (no published Sistani or Khamenei ruling; Iran's Qom seminary partners with AI research centers while Khamenei promotes AI development as state strategy).

**Fault lines.** Gulf tech-nationalism versus traditionalist caution — the UAE's AI minister Omar Al Olama explicitly invokes the Ottoman rejection of the printing press as the cautionary tale against religious gatekeeping; "corpus anxiety" (who trains the model that speaks about God?) is the most consistent Sunni red line, visible identically in NU's own-corpus demand and Dar al-Ifta's comprehension argument; and AI has become a new arena of the old Sunni authority competition among Al-Azhar, the Gulf councils, and the Southeast Asian mass organizations.

**Persuasion levers.** *Maslaha/maqasid* (public benefit within the five protected interests) is the institutionally legible frame — it is the IIFA resolution's own logic. *Amanah* (trusteeship) maps onto accountability and transparency; *khalifa* (stewardship) onto human-in-the-loop requirements; *la darar* (no harm) onto restrictions of deepfakes and religious misinformation. The strongest lever with scholars is **'ilm and isnad**: AI outputs lack a chain of transmission, so source-transparent, scholar-audited AI converts opponents — exactly what NU proposed. The printing-press analogy is potent with modernizing elites but is wielded by both sides.

### 5.5 Judaism (~15 million, with influence far beyond its numbers in AI itself)

**Core beliefs and canonical texts.** Jewish thought possesses the oldest continuous tradition of reasoning about artificial persons: the golem. The talmudic locus is **Sanhedrin 65b** (Rava creates a man; it cannot speak; Rabbi Zeira returns it to dust) — speech and *da'at* (intellect/intention) as the personhood test — refined in **Hakham Tzvi's Responsum #93** (early 18th century): a golem does not count in a minyan. This is the standard precedent cited for "can AI count for ritual purposes" (no: no soul, not born of a mother, no obligation in the commandments). The tradition's modern hinge is Gershom Scholem's dedication address for the Weizmann Institute's computer "Golem Aleph" (June 17, 1965; published as "The Golem of Prague and the Golem of Rehovoth," *Commentary*, January 1966), closing: "develop peacefully and don't destroy the world. Shalom" — alongside Norbert Wiener's *God and Golem, Inc.* (MIT Press, 1964). The most substantive Jewish legal text on AI is Rabbi Daniel Nevins, **"Halakhic Responses to Artificial Intelligence and Autonomous Machines"** (Conservative movement CJLS, approved June 19, 2019): humans remain liable for machine actions; halakhically derived ethics should inform autonomous-systems design; humans must retain responsibility for life-and-death decisions; an artificial agent is not a person and cannot perform mitzvot. The Reform CCAR issued Responsum 5784.2, "Artificial Intelligence and Authorship" (2025). The Chief Rabbinate of Israel signed the Rome Call (via Rabbi Eliezer Simha Weisz, January 10, 2023) — after which Weisz himself published a critique, "The Illusion of Algor-Ethics," arguing the Vatican avoided the golem's lesson: creation requires responsibility, not just principles.

**Gatekeepers and fault lines.** The CJLS and CCAR responsa committees; Orthodox poskim (Rav Asher Weiss has ruled AI "decisions" are mechanical causation because AI lacks *da'at*); the Zomet Institute (whose *gerama* — indirect causation — framework for Shabbat technology is the template for automation questions); Chabad's popular publishing; Sefaria and interfaith brokers (Sinai and Synapses). Fault lines: Orthodox case-by-case pragmatism versus haredi gatekept adoption (the "kosher phone" precedent predicts filtered AI); liberal movements framing AI as ethics and justice versus Orthodox framing as ritual status and liability; universal consensus that "ChatGPT cannot be a posek" alongside enthusiastic adoption of AI for Torah study.

**Persuasion levers.** *Pikuach nefesh* (saving life overrides nearly everything — medical AI is the easiest yes in all of Judaism); *b'tselem Elohim* (anti-dehumanization); *emet* and the speech-ethics of *geneivat da'at* and *lashon hara* (deepfakes and misinformation map directly onto guarding truthful speech); Shabbat as a ready-made discipline of technological restraint; and the golem narrative itself — the tradition's native way of saying "build with a kill-switch."

### 5.6 Hinduism (~1.2 billion)

**Core beliefs.** Hinduism complicates every Western assumption that religiosity predicts AI-aversion. Three doctrinal resources cushion technological ritual: **murti** worship (consecrated images as loci of divine presence via *prana pratishtha*), **Ayudha Puja** (the annual worship of one's tools — now explicitly including laptops and servers), and **Vishwakarma Puja** (honoring the divine architect; machines garlanded on factory floors — the AI engineer as Vishwakarma's child). Ritual automation is already practice, not speculation: a robotic arm performed Ganesh aarti at Pune's Ganeshotsav in 2017, and PETA India has donated five life-size robotic elephants to South Indian temples since the ceremonial dedication of "Irinjadappilly Raman" (Kerala, February 26, 2023) — technology adoption successfully marketed as *ahimsa* (compassion). At the same time, Advaita Vedanta denies machine consciousness on principled grounds: consciousness is the substrate of reality, not an emergent function — the position argued publicly by Swami **Sarvapriyananda** (Vedanta Society of New York) in dialogues with David Chalmers and, in 2025, with Google's Blaise Agüera y Arcas. The key scholarly works are Holly Walters's studies of robotic ritual (The Conversation, 2023 — devotees fear being replaced *as worshippers*, not just as priests) and Robert Geraci and Stephen Kaplan, "Hinduism and Artificial Intelligence," in *The Cambridge Companion to Religion and Artificial Intelligence* (Cambridge, 2024). The cautionary case is **GitaGPT** (2023): Gita chatbots speaking "as Krishna" that condoned violence as dharma — mythologist Devdutt Pattanaik's public debunking made scripture-chatbot authority a live anxiety.

**Gatekeepers and fault lines.** There is no magisterium; authority is radically dispersed — temple trusts and devaswom boards, matha heads (no Shankaracharya has issued an AI statement), ISKCON's devotee-built preaching bots (no institutional ruling), diaspora advocacy organizations that engage AI as a *representation* issue (anti-Hinduphobia bias detection), and celebrity gurus. Indian state discourse (Modi's "human-centric AI," the 2026 India AI Impact Summit's civilizational framing, top-3-AI-power-by-2047 goals) is Hindu-inflected tech-nationalism rather than doctrine, and it is the single strongest opinion force among Hindus globally. Fault lines: Hindutva tech-nationalism versus ritual purists; priest livelihoods versus automation; chatbot authority anxiety.

**Persuasion levers.** *Dharma/svadharma* of the technologist (building well as duty); *seva* (AI as service to devotees); *ahimsa* (the robotic-elephant success template); *jnana* (Vedantic prestige framing: AI clarifies what consciousness is *not*, reaffirming atman); and the Ayudha Puja/Vishwakarma frame — tools become sacred when consecrated to right work. For mass audiences, national-standing arguments outperform all religious ones.

### 5.7 Buddhism (~500 million)

**Core beliefs.** Buddhism's non-self doctrine (*anatta*) lowers the metaphysical bar for machine minds — personhood is already an aggregate process — but its criterion of moral standing is the capacity to suffer (*dukkha*), not computation, and its theory of moral weight tracks intention (*cetana*): "Intention, monks, is what I call karma" (AN 6.63). An AI without intention generates no karma; responsibility falls entirely on designers and deployers — a natively Buddhist argument for builder accountability. The two book-length foundations are Peter D. Hershock, *Buddhism and Intelligent Technology: Toward a More Humane Future* (Bloomsbury, 2021 — the attention economy as "colonization of consciousness") and Soraj Hongladarom, *The Ethics of AI and Robotics: A Buddhist Viewpoint* (Lexington, 2020 — "machine enlightenment" as design standard). The **Dalai Lama's** record is deliberately double: in 2023 he stated AI cannot have consciousness, yet in the Varela-era Mind & Life dialogues he declined to "totally rule out" that a stream of consciousness could associate with a computer given the karmic conditions; the Mind & Life Institute's Dialogue XXXIX ("Minds, Artificial Intelligence and Ethics," Dharamsala, 2025) distilled his formula: intelligence must be paired with "warm-heartedness."

Institutional Asia is strikingly comfortable with robot ritual, eased by *upaya* (skillful means) and priest-shortage economics: the android Kannon **Mindar** delivers Heart Sutra sermons at Kyoto's Kodai-ji temple (unveiled 2019, built with Hiroshi Ishiguro's Osaka University lab); SoftBank's Pepper was programmed to chant funeral sutras (2017); Beijing's Longquan Monastery ran the robot monk Xian'er (2015–16); Kyoto University's **BuddhaBot** partnered with Bhutan's Central Monastic Body (2021–25); and Korea's **Jogye Order** — the most institutionally proactive Buddhist body — pledged to "fearlessly lead the A.I. era," fielded an AI counseling chatbot, and in 2026 ordained the robot monk "Gabi" with the statement that "technology must be used in accordance with the values of compassion, wisdom, and responsibility." Against this, field experiments show robot preachers *reduce* religious commitment and donations (Jackson et al., "Exposure to Robot Preachers Undermines Religious Commitment," *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General*, 2023 — run partly on Mindar itself). Western convert Buddhism engages through the Silicon Valley interface: Jack Kornfield's Wisdom 2.0 dialogue with Sam Altman (2023) — "We are the nation of nuclear giants and ethical infants."

**Persuasion levers.** *Karuna* (compassion as a design requirement — a robot bodhisattva is literally compassion-as-interface); *ahimsa* (first-precept framing for AI safety); interdependence (*pratityasamutpada* — systemic-risk framing lands natively; Buddhism is the tradition most receptive to AI-safety arguments as stated); attention ethics (the attention economy as institutionalized craving); and *cetana* (responsibility tracks intention — accountability of builders). The fault line to respect: Asian institutional comfort with ritual automation versus Western teachers' insistence that AI lacks lived realization.

### 5.8 Secular Humanism (contrast case)

Organized humanism formalized its position in the **Luxembourg Declaration on Artificial Intelligence and Human Values** (Humanists International General Assembly, July 6, 2025): ten rights-based principles — democratic accountability, transparency, protection from algorithmic discrimination, fair compensation for creators, human control of consequential decisions, AI for flourishing rather than power concentration. The instructive contrast: humanists produce centralized deliberative declarations grounded in human-rights law; Hindu authority, at the other extreme, expresses itself through ritual practice adoption with no declarations at all. Persuasion levers: empirical evidence, rights law, flourishing metrics, anti-concentration arguments — and a standing wariness of *both* techno-utopianism and doomerism as quasi-religious.

### 5.9 What the Religion Data Say Quantitatively

The best empirical anchors: Pew Research Center, *AI and Human Enhancement* (March 2022) — 81% of Americans high in religious commitment say cognitive-enhancement brain chips would be "meddling with nature and crossing a line," 72% say the same of gene-editing babies; Pew (September 2025) — 73% of Americans say AI should play *no role* in advising people about faith in God, the highest "no role" of any surveyed domain; Barna/Gloo (2024–26) — pastors are more cautious than laity (71% vs. 36% "cautious") yet 77% of pastors agree God can work through AI, and pastoral AI use roughly tripled in two years; Lifeway Research (April 2026) — 43% of Protestant churchgoers object to AI in sermon preparation. The through-line: religiosity predicts wariness specifically in the *body, soul, and authority* domains while leaving instrumental adoption largely free — which is exactly where the doctrinal documents above draw their lines.
