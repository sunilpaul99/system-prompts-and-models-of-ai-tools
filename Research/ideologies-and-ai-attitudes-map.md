## 1. Introduction: Purpose and Method

This document maps the world's major ideologies and belief groups onto their views of artificial intelligence. It is written for a reader who wants to *influence* the opinions of these groups — which means each entry must answer five questions: What does this group believe at its core? What are its canonical texts? How do those beliefs translate into attitudes toward AI (existential risk, jobs, human dignity, openness, regulation)? Who are the gatekeepers who move opinion inside the group? And what persuasion levers work, given the group's own moral vocabulary?

Two structural facts organize everything that follows.

**First, the people who build AI and the people who will live with it belong to almost entirely disjoint belief communities.** The ideologies that dominate AI research and industry — effective altruism, rationalism, effective accelerationism, transhumanism, the critical AI-ethics school — together count perhaps a few hundred thousand adherents worldwide, yet they have set the terms of the entire AI debate: the words "alignment," "x-risk," "AI safety," "responsible AI," and "acceleration" all come from these micro-ideologies. Meanwhile the world's large belief groups — 2.4 billion Christians, 1.9 billion Muslims, 1.2 billion Hindus, half a billion Buddhists, and the great political blocs — are only now developing formal positions on AI, and they are doing so in their own moral vocabularies, which the AI world largely does not speak. The influence opportunity lies precisely in this translation gap.

**Second, attitudes toward AI are not yet politically "frozen."** Unlike climate change or vaccination, AI has not yet been fully absorbed into existing partisan or sectarian identity structures. Survey data (Section 6) show AI anxiety crossing party, class, and confessional lines in unusual ways — American conservatives and progressive AI-ethics scholars both distrust Big Tech; Catholic bishops and secular labor unions both center human dignity and work. Coalitions are still available across normally hostile borders. Every case study in Section 7 (SB-1047, the EU AI Act, the open-weights fight, the OpenAI board crisis) shows opinion moving quickly when the right messenger used the right frame. This window will close as AI politics polarizes; the groups mapped here are still persuadable.

**Method.** This synthesis was produced through a multi-agent deep-research process: parallel search sweeps across (a) belief-evaluation frameworks, (b) the elite ideologies of the AI industry, (c) large political ideologies, (d) religious traditions (a dedicated five-track deep dive), and (e) empirical survey data and documented cases of opinion change — followed by adversarial verification of claims and citations. A verification caveat applies throughout: the research environment permitted web *search* corroboration across multiple independent sources but blocked direct fetching of most primary documents; citations below were each corroborated by two or more independent sources, and known misattributions discovered during verification are flagged inline. Citations to pre-2023 canonical works (Bostrom, Singer, Haidt, Douglas & Wildavsky, etc.) are standard published editions.

## 2. Frameworks for Evaluating Beliefs

No single lens organizes "belief groups and AI" adequately. Five frameworks are evaluated here; the practical recommendation is to use them in layers — Moral Foundations to craft the message, Cultural Theory to predict the risk reflex, Inglehart–Welzel to locate the society, the AI-tribes taxonomy to navigate the industry, and TESCREAL only to understand one influential critique.

### 2.1 Moral Foundations Theory (MFT)

**Original works:** Jonathan Haidt and Jesse Graham, "When Morality Opposes Justice: Conservatives Have Moral Intuitions that Liberals May Not Recognize," *Social Justice Research* 20 (2007); Jesse Graham, Jonathan Haidt, and Brian Nosek, "Liberals and Conservatives Rely on Different Sets of Moral Foundations," *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology* 96:5 (2009); Jonathan Haidt, *The Righteous Mind: Why Good People Are Divided by Politics and Religion* (Pantheon, 2012).

MFT holds that moral judgment rests on a small set of intuitive foundations — care/harm, fairness/cheating, loyalty/betrayal, authority/subversion, sanctity/degradation (later liberty/oppression) — and that political liberals moralize mainly the first two while conservatives moralize all of them. Its value for AI persuasion is direct: the same AI policy can be argued as *harm prevention* (progressives), as *protection of the sacred human person* (religious conservatives — note how precisely the Vatican's and the Southern Baptists' documents in Section 5 track the sanctity foundation), as *loyalty to nation and workers* (populists), or as *liberty against concentrated power* (libertarians — and note that anti-Big-Tech liberty framing is currently the only frame that reaches all four quadrants at once). MFT's weakness: it describes moral *style*, not doctrinal content; it cannot tell you what a Muslim jurist or a rationalist forecaster actually believes about machine agency. Use it to tune rhetoric, not to predict positions.

### 2.2 Cultural Theory of Risk and Cultural Cognition

**Original works:** Mary Douglas and Aaron Wildavsky, *Risk and Culture: An Essay on the Selection of Technological and Environmental Dangers* (University of California Press, 1982); Dan M. Kahan et al., "Culture and Identity-Protective Cognition: Explaining the White-Male Effect in Risk Perception," *Journal of Empirical Legal Studies* 4:3 (2007); Dan M. Kahan, "Cultural Cognition as a Conception of the Cultural Theory of Risk," in *Handbook of Risk Theory* (Springer, 2012).

Douglas and Wildavsky's central claim — that societies *select* which dangers to fear according to their form of social organization (hierarchical, individualist, egalitarian, fatalist) — is the single best predictor of how a group will process AI risk claims. Egalitarians (the AI-ethics school, the labor left) fear technologies that entrench corporate power and inequality; individualists (techno-libertarians, e/acc) discount technological risk and fear regulation itself; hierarchists (the Vatican, the CCP, national-security conservatives) fear disorder and loss of authoritative control — which is why both the Holy See and the Chinese state, from opposite metaphysics, converge on "human/state control over AI." Kahan's cultural-cognition research adds the operational lesson: people accept risk information from messengers who share their values and reject the same information from outsiders — *the messenger is the message*. This is the deepest framework for the influence use-case, and it explains the empirical strangeness documented in Sections 6–7 (e.g., evangelical wariness of AI aligning with secular AI-safety doomerism against libertarian optimism).

### 2.3 Inglehart–Welzel Values Dimensions

**Original works:** Ronald Inglehart, *The Silent Revolution: Changing Values and Political Styles Among Western Publics* (Princeton, 1977); Ronald Inglehart and Christian Welzel, *Modernization, Cultural Change, and Democracy: The Human Development Sequence* (Cambridge, 2005); the World Values Survey cultural map (worldvaluessurvey.org).

The WVS maps societies on two axes: traditional vs. secular-rational values, and survival vs. self-expression values. Its predictive use for AI: societies high in survival values (much of the Global South, post-Soviet space) evaluate AI primarily through jobs, security, and national standing; societies high in self-expression values (Protestant Europe, Anglosphere) evaluate it through autonomy, privacy, and rights. Cross-national polling (Ipsos, *Global Views on A.I.*, annual since 2022) consistently finds emerging-market publics far more AI-optimistic than rich-country publics — a pattern Inglehart–Welzel predicts and that most Western AI discourse ignores. Weakness: nation-level resolution; useless for intra-society persuasion.

### 2.4 The AI-Tribes Taxonomy (Doomers, Accelerationists, Ethicists, Pragmatists)

**Representative original works:** for the "doomer" pole, Eliezer Yudkowsky, "Pausing AI Developments Isn't Enough. We Need to Shut it All Down," *TIME*, March 29, 2023; for the accelerationist pole, Marc Andreessen, "The Techno-Optimist Manifesto" (a16z.com, October 16, 2023); for the ethics pole, Emily M. Bender, Timnit Gebru, Angelina McMillan-Major, and Margaret Mitchell (as "Shmargaret Shmitchell"), "On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots: Can Language Models Be Too Big? 🦜," *FAccT '21* (ACM, 2021); for the pragmatist center, the corporate "responsible scaling" literature (e.g., Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy, 2023).

This journalistic taxonomy — it crystallized in 2023 coverage of the ChatGPT moment — is the working map *inside* the AI industry, and Section 3 is organized around a refined version of it. Its virtue is currency; its vice is that it flattens deep ideological genealogies (EA utilitarianism, LessWrong epistemics, Landian accelerationism, critical race theory and STS in the FAccT school) into Twitter factions, and it has near-zero resolution outside the industry. Use it for navigating AI-researcher and lab-leader opinion; do not project it onto publics.

### 2.5 TESCREAL

**Original work:** Timnit Gebru and Émile P. Torres, "The TESCREAL bundle: Eugenics and the promise of utopia through artificial general intelligence," *First Monday* 29:4 (April 2024).

Gebru and Torres bundle Transhumanism, Extropianism, Singularitarianism, Cosmism, Rationalism, Effective Altruism, and Longtermism into a single lineage ("TESCREAL") which they trace to twentieth-century eugenics, arguing that the AGI project itself is the bundle's utopian program. As analysis, the bundle is contested — it groups movements that fight each other bitterly (EA safetyism vs. e/acc), and critics note the genealogical argument does more guilt-by-association than mechanism-tracing. But as a *political fact* it matters enormously: TESCREAL is the organizing frame through which the academic left, much of tech journalism, and parts of the policy world now interpret the entire AI-safety enterprise. Anyone seeking to influence progressive, academic, or civil-society audiences must know this frame; anyone seeking to influence EA/rationalist audiences must know they experience it as a slur. It is a lens to be understood, not adopted.

## 3. The Elite Ideologies of the AI World

These movements are tiny — none plausibly exceeds the low hundreds of thousands of identifiers, and the committed cores are in the thousands — but they staff the labs, fund the field, write the manifestos, and set the vocabulary in which everyone else must argue. They are best understood as one family tree with a civil war inside it, plus one rival school outside it.

### 3.1 Effective Altruism and Longtermism — the anchor case

**History and canonical texts.** EA begins in analytic moral philosophy: Peter Singer, "Famine, Affluence, and Morality," *Philosophy & Public Affairs* 1:3 (1972) — the argument that distance does not diminish the duty to prevent suffering at trivial cost to ourselves. The movement institutionalized around 2009–2011 (Toby Ord's Giving What We Can; William MacAskill's Centre for Effective Altruism; the GiveWell/Open Philanthropy charity-evaluation apparatus), with MacAskill's *Doing Good Better* (2015) as the recruiting text. The **longtermist turn** rests on Nick Bostrom, "Existential Risk Prevention as Global Priority," *Global Policy* 4:1 (2013), and *Superintelligence: Paths, Dangers, Strategies* (Oxford, 2014); Toby Ord, *The Precipice: Existential Risk and the Future of Humanity* (2020); and MacAskill, *What We Owe the Future* (2022). Eliezer Yudkowsky supplied the alignment problem's founding corpus (the LessWrong "Sequences," 2006–09; MIRI's technical agenda) and its most radical public statement: "Pausing AI Developments Isn't Enough. We Need to Shut it All Down," *TIME*, March 29, 2023. A notable critical account (Mollie Gleiberman, University of Antwerp working paper, 2023 — *flagged: could not be adversarially verified in this research pass*) argues the longtermist agenda was not a "turn" at all but present from the founding, with poverty-focused messaging serving as the public face — a "two-level discourse" thesis that EA's critics now widely cite.

**Influence on AI-safety funding and lab culture.** This is where EA punches furthest above its weight. Open Philanthropy (Holden Karnofsky) became the dominant funder of AI-safety research; Karnofsky sat on OpenAI's four-person board by March 2017 alongside Musk and Altman, attached to Open Philanthropy's $30M grant. Anthropic was founded (2021) by safety-focused OpenAI alumni with substantial EA-aligned backing; 80,000 Hours channels elite graduates into "AI safety careers"; the terms "alignment," "x-risk," and "p(doom)" are all EA/rationalist coinage that now structure even their opponents' speech. The empirical footprint is visible in the AI Impacts expert survey data in Section 6: a majority of published AI researchers now assign ≥5% probability to extinction-level outcomes, and 70% say safety research should be prioritized more.

**The two traumas.** EA's public standing was broken twice in twelve months: the collapse of FTX (November 2022), whose founder Sam Bankman-Fried was EA's most prominent donor and used explicitly EA justifications; and the OpenAI board crisis (November 2023 — Section 7.4), universally narrated as an "EA coup" that failed. The backlash formalized on two fronts: e/acc (3.3), which treats EA safetyism as civilizational cowardice, and the TESCREAL critique (2.5), which treats it as eugenics-descended utopianism. EA now operates with damaged brand but intact infrastructure — the funding, career pipeline, and policy staff survived the reputational collapse.

**Gatekeepers.** MacAskill, Ord, Karnofsky (now writing on AI futures), Dustin Moskovitz (funding), Yudkowsky (doomer pole), the leadership of Open Philanthropy, 80,000 Hours, and the Constellation/Lightcone research ecosystem; within labs, the safety teams of Anthropic, OpenAI, and DeepMind are heavily EA-socialized.

**Persuasion levers.** EAs respond to expected-value arguments, quantified uncertainty, and cause-prioritization logic ("importance, tractability, neglectedness"); they update publicly and reward interlocutors who state credences and accept bets. Moral-authority, tradition, or identity appeals fail completely. The efficient frame for moving EAs is *marginal impact*: show that a proposed action buys more risk-reduction per dollar or per unit of political capital than the current portfolio. Conversely, when EA messengers address outside audiences they are now a liability — the FTX and OpenAI narratives arrive before the argument does.

### 3.2 Rationalism / LessWrong

The parent culture of EA-safetyism. **Canonical texts:** Yudkowsky's Sequences (collected as *Rationality: From AI to Zombies*, 2015); Scott Alexander's *Slate Star Codex* / *Astral Codex Ten* essays. Core commitments: Bayesian epistemics as a way of life, the alignment problem as humanity's central intellectual challenge, distrust of credentialed authority in favor of argument quality, and a distinctive discourse culture (steelmanning, crux-finding, calibration). Rationalists are the intellectual engine room of the doomer pole but also of much anti-doomer argument — the community prizes disagreement. **Levers:** identical to EA plus one: rationalists are moved by *forecasting track records*. Prediction-market evidence (Metaculus, Manifold) and calibrated-forecaster endorsements carry more weight than institutional or expert authority. The fastest way to lose a rationalist audience is appeal to consensus or social proof.

### 3.3 Effective Accelerationism (e/acc)

**Canonical texts:** the pseudonymous "Beff Jezos" (Guillaume Verdon) and bayeslord's e/acc Substack posts ("Notes on e/acc principles and tenets," 2022), which ground the movement in thermodynamics — civilization as an entropy-dissipating process whose duty is to climb the Kardashev scale by maximizing energy capture, with AGI as the necessary instrument; and Marc Andreessen's **"The Techno-Optimist Manifesto"** (a16z.com, October 16, 2023, ~5,200 words), the movement's mainstream charter, which names as "enemies" the ideas of social responsibility, trust and safety, sustainability, ESG, and tech ethics — a declaration of war on both the safety and ethics camps at once. The deeper genealogy runs to Nick Land and the 1990s CCRU's accelerationism, though most e/acc adherents know the movement through Twitter/X, where Andreessen and Y Combinator's Garry Tan carried "e/acc" in their bios. Reception split the tech elite on cue: Shopify's Tobias Lütke, Coinbase's Brian Armstrong, and Steven Sinofsky praised the manifesto; critics fixated on its approving citation of Marinetti's Futurist Manifesto.

**Beliefs → AI attitudes:** regulation is civilizational self-harm; open proliferation is good; existential-risk claims are either sincere errors or deliberate moats for incumbents ("regulatory capture by doomerism"); the moral valence of building is unconditionally positive. **Gatekeepers:** Andreessen and the a16z partner network, Verdon, Tan, and the builder-Twitter attention economy. **Levers:** e/acc identity is *builder* identity — arguments framed as "this rule stops builders" land; arguments framed as harm-prevention are read as enemy speech. The two openings: (1) national-competition framing (e/acc is heavily American-nationalist in practice; "China wins if..." moves them), and (2) energy — anything that accelerates energy abundance buys credibility that can be spent elsewhere. There is no lever that makes e/acc accept ex-ante safety regulation; the achievable outcome is narrowing what they mobilize against.

### 3.4 Transhumanism and Extropianism

The elder tradition the others grew from. **Canonical texts:** Max More, "The Extropian Principles" (v1 1990; *Principles of Extropy* 3.11, 2003); Ray Kurzweil, *The Singularity Is Near* (2005); Nick Bostrom, "Transhumanist Values" (2003). Core commitments: morphological freedom, indefinite lifespan, intelligence enhancement, and the desirability of a post-biological future. As an organized movement it has largely dissolved into its descendants — singularitarian optimism into e/acc and Kurzweil's corner of Google, and risk-aware transhumanism into EA-longtermism — but its vocabulary ("uploading," "posthuman") still defines what religious critics (Sections 5.1, 5.3) are *against*: LDS embodiment doctrine, Catholic anthropology, and evangelical imago Dei statements all draw their lines explicitly against transhumanist aspirations. **Lever:** for the remaining core, longevity — medical-AI acceleration is the offer they cannot refuse.

### 3.5 The AI Ethics / FAccT School

The rival school. **Canonical texts:** Emily M. Bender, Timnit Gebru, Angelina McMillan-Major, and Margaret Mitchell, "On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots: Can Language Models Be Too Big? 🦜" (*FAccT '21*) — the paper whose suppression triggered Gebru's exit from Google (December 2020) and made her the school's central figure; Safiya Umoja Noble, *Algorithms of Oppression* (NYU Press, 2018); Cathy O'Neil, *Weapons of Math Destruction* (2016); Ruha Benjamin, *Race After Technology* (Polity, 2019); Kate Crawford, *Atlas of AI* (Yale, 2021); Joy Buolamwini and Gebru, "Gender Shades" (*FAccT/PMLR*, 2018); and the TESCREAL paper itself (2.5). Institutional bases: the FAccT conference, DAIR (Gebru's institute), AI Now Institute, Algorithmic Justice League.

**Beliefs → AI attitudes:** AI's harms are *present*, *distributional*, and *structural* — bias, surveillance, labor extraction, environmental cost, colonial data practices; "existential risk" is a distraction that re-centers the powerful and hypothetical future people over harmed present ones (the core dividing line with the safety camp, stated exactly in the TESCREAL paper). Pro-regulation, but for accountability and civil-rights enforcement rather than catastrophe prevention; deeply skeptical of both closed corporate AI *and* safety arguments for closure. **Gatekeepers:** Gebru, Bender, Mitchell, Noble, Benjamin, Buolamwini, Crawford, Meredith Whittaker (Signal), Deborah Raji; amplified through tech journalism and academic networks far exceeding the school's headcount. **Levers:** documented material harm to identifiable communities is the only fully legitimate currency; participatory framing ("nothing about us without us") governs who may speak. The bridge opportunity: the AI Impacts data (Section 6) show rank-and-file researchers hold *both* schools' concerns simultaneously — misinformation, authoritarian control, and inequality worry majorities alongside extinction — so "portfolio" framings that fund both agendas can recruit the middle even though the elite poles treat the pairing as betrayal.

### 3.6 Open-Source Techno-Libertarianism

**Canonical texts:** John Perry Barlow, "A Declaration of the Independence of Cyberspace" (1996) — the ancestral document; Eric S. Raymond, *The Cathedral and the Bazaar* (1999); and for the AI era, Mark Zuckerberg, "Open Source AI Is the Path Forward" (Meta, July 2024), published with Llama 3.1 405B — described by TIME as the first open-weights model at the capability frontier — and publicly praised by Musk and Jack Dorsey. Yann LeCun is the school's chief scientist-advocate: openness as both innovation engine and *safety mechanism* (many eyes, no single point of control). Opposed by safety-side figures — FLI's policy staff warning that "the most authoritarian states will likely repurpose models like Llama," Control AI's Andrea Miotti accusing Meta of "disregard for basic security."

**Beliefs → AI attitudes:** anti-monopoly, anti-gatekeeping, pro-proliferation; regulation acceptable only at the application layer, never on model weights or compute. **Gatekeepers:** LeCun, Zuckerberg's Meta AI organization, Hugging Face (Clément Delangue), a16z's open-source advocacy, EleutherAI/Mistral communities, EFF. **Levers:** freedom-to-compute and anti-concentration arguments; security-through-transparency evidence. This school is the natural coalition partner of e/acc on regulation fights (SB-1047) but splits from it on corporate power — open-source advocates distrust *all* concentrated control, including Andreessen's portfolio companies. That seam is workable.

### 3.7 Degrowth and the Left Tech Critique

**Canonical texts:** Shoshana Zuboff, *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* (2019); Harry Braverman, *Labor and Monopoly Capital* (1974) — the deskilling thesis every AI-labor argument descends from, itself descending from Marx's "Fragment on Machines" (*Grundrisse*, 1857–58); Evgeny Morozov's anti-solutionism (*To Save Everything, Click Here*, 2013); and the ecological critique of compute (data-center energy and water studies). Institutional carriers: unions — the 2023 WGA strike won the first major collectively-bargained AI clauses (AI cannot write or rewrite literary material; AI output is not "source material"), and SAG-AFTRA won consent-and-compensation rules for digital replicas — plus Tech Won't Build It-style worker organizing. **Beliefs → AI attitudes:** AI as the latest enclosure — of labor value, attention, and public data; the question is never capability but *ownership*. **Levers:** who-profits analysis, bargaining-table wins as the template (the WGA clause is the most-cited proof that resistance produces results), public/cooperative ownership proposals, and environmental cost. This bloc supplies the mass-politics translation of the FAccT school's academic critique.

### 3.8 What AI Researchers Actually Believe

The rank and file are not members of any of these movements, but survey data show which way the ideological winds blow through them. The 2023 AI Impacts survey (Katja Grace et al., "Thousands of AI Authors on the Future of AI" — 2,778 researchers published in six top venues, fielded October 2023, the largest expert survey conducted) found: aggregate forecasts of a 10% chance of machines outperforming humans at *every* task by 2027 and 50% by 2047 — thirteen years earlier than the same panel's estimate just one year prior, the sharpest documented post-ChatGPT belief shift; a majority assigning ≥5% probability to human extinction or permanent disempowerment (median 5%; 38–51% gave ≥10% depending on framing); 70% saying AI-safety research should be prioritized more; and majorities expressing substantial concern about misinformation, authoritarian population control, and inequality — i.e., *the median researcher holds the doomer and the ethicist concerns simultaneously*, which neither elite pole admits is coherent. (The earlier baseline is Zhang et al., "Ethics and Governance of Artificial Intelligence: Evidence from a Survey of Machine Learning Researchers," *JAIR* 71, 2021.) Pew's parallel surveys (April 2025: 1,013 AI experts vs. 5,410 US adults) add the social profile: experts are far more optimistic than the public (56% vs. 17% expect positive impact), yet a majority of experts *joins* the public in wanting more personal control and distrusting government's regulatory competence — and expert optimism itself splits sharply by gender (63% of male experts positive vs. 36% of female experts), which partially explains why the safety/ethics camps, with very different gender compositions, perceive different technologies.

## 4. Large Political Ideologies and AI

### 4.1 US Progressivism

Progressive AI politics is the FAccT school's mass form: civil-rights enforcement, algorithmic accountability, worker protection, and platform distrust, canonized in the White House OSTP's *Blueprint for an AI Bill of Rights* (October 2022). Gatekeepers: congressional tech critics, civil-rights organizations (Leadership Conference, ACLU), unions, and the ethics-research network. Levers (per MFT): care/harm and fairness dominate — discrimination evidence, worker displacement, and corporate-power framing all land; existential-risk framing lands only when routed through "unaccountable corporations are gambling with everyone's safety." Note from Pew (June 2025): Democrats and Republicans are now *equally* concerned about AI in daily life (51% vs. 50%) — concern is bipartisan; the divergence is remedy, with Democrats more worried regulation won't go far enough (64% vs. 55%).

### 4.2 Conservatism and National Conservatism

Three currents compete. **Anti-Big-Tech populism** (censorship grievance, "woke AI" — the animating charge that frontier models encode progressive values; carried into the 2025 administration's AI executive actions and procurement rules). **China-hawk nationalism** — the strongest single force: AI as the decisive theater of great-power competition, favoring acceleration of American capability and export controls over safety regulation. **Religious-social conservatism** — human dignity, family, and children-online concerns, increasingly supplied with vocabulary by the documents in Section 5. Gatekeepers: movement media, national-security think tanks, evangelical policy bodies (ERLC), and — decisively since 2024 — the tech right (Andreessen, Musk, David Sacks) which grafted e/acc onto the coalition. Levers: liberty/oppression (anti-Big-Tech antitrust framing is the rare message that unites the tech right's enemies with the populist base *against* the tech right), authority/subversion (AI undermining parental and national authority), and sanctity (via the religious wing). Republicans trust US regulatory capacity more than Democrats do (54% vs. 36%, Pew 2025) — a reversal of the usual valence, driven by who holds power.

### 4.3 Socialism and the Labor Left

Canonical lineage: Marx's "Fragment on Machines" through Braverman (3.7). Contemporary form: bargaining over AI as a working condition. The WGA and SAG-AFTRA outcomes (2023) are the movement's proof-of-concept and its persuasion template: AI attitudes here are not metaphysical but contractual — what may be automated, who consents, who is compensated. Gatekeepers: union leadership (SAG-AFTRA's Duncan Crabtree-Ireland became the face of AI bargaining), the AFL-CIO's technology institute, and left media. Levers: "AI decisions belong at the bargaining table"; ownership and compensation proposals; solidarity framing across white-collar and blue-collar automation. This bloc is structurally open to alliance with safety advocates on *slowing deployment* and with conservatives on *anti-Big-Tech* measures — the most under-exploited coalition seam in AI politics.

### 4.4 Libertarianism

Canonical texts: Hayek, "The Use of Knowledge in Society" (*American Economic Review*, 1945) — decentralized knowledge as the argument against planned AI governance; the "permissionless innovation" literature (Adam Thierer, 2014). AI attitudes: regulation is the risk; markets and liability law suffice; compute thresholds are central planning. Gatekeepers: Cato, Mercatus, R Street, and the overlap with open-source advocacy and e/acc. Levers: state-failure evidence, competition policy framed as deregulation, and civil-liberties alarm at surveillance uses of *government* AI (the one pro-restriction argument libertarians natively make).

### 4.5 Populist Nationalism (US, Europe, India, and beyond)

Across countries, the populist AI synthesis is consistent: pro-AI as national power, anti-AI as elite instrument. Jobs anxiety fuses with sovereignty anxiety — foreign models as cultural colonization (the same "corpus anxiety" documented among religious authorities in Section 5.4 appears in secular nationalist form as "sovereign AI": India's national AI mission and civilizational framing at the 2026 India AI Impact Summit; Gulf state AI nationalism; European digital-sovereignty discourse; Russia's "traditional values" AI). Levers: national-champion framing, data and compute sovereignty, and protection of national workers; universalist safety arguments read as globalist and fail. The Ipsos data (Section 6) matter here: emerging-market publics are the world's most AI-optimistic *and* most government-trusting on AI — populist elites in those countries face pro-AI publics, the opposite of the Anglosphere configuration.

### 4.6 Chinese Techno-Statism

Canonical documents: the State Council's *New Generation Artificial Intelligence Development Plan* (July 2017 — AI leadership by 2030 as national strategy); the *Interim Measures for the Management of Generative AI Services* (CAC, 2023), which require alignment with "core socialist values"; the *Global AI Governance Initiative* (2023) exporting a sovereignty-first governance model; and the Beijing AI Principles (2019, Yi Zeng), which wrap governance in harmony vocabulary with Confucian resonance. Beliefs → attitudes: AI is a tool of national rejuvenation and social stability; "safety" means political controllability first, catastrophe prevention second (though Chinese signatories have joined international x-risk statements, and US–China track-two dialogue on frontier risk exists); openness is instrumental (Chinese open-weights releases as soft power). Gatekeepers: the CAC, MOST, and party science policy; Yi Zeng and academic brokers for international audiences. Levers: stability, sovereignty, development rights, and great-civilization status; any frame implying Western moral tutelage fails. Chinese publics are among the world's most AI-optimistic (Ipsos), giving the state a permissive environment its Western counterparts lack.

### 4.7 EU Regulatory Liberalism

The EU's ideology of AI is proceduralist rights-protection projected globally — the "Brussels effect" (Anu Bradford, *The Brussels Effect*, Columbia, 2020) — expressed in the AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689), the GDPR's successor logic of risk-tiered product regulation. Gatekeepers: the Commission (DG CNECT), Parliament rapporteurs, and national digital ministries — but Section 7.2 shows national champions (Mistral, Aleph Alpha) can flip member states against Brussels within weeks. Levers: fundamental-rights vocabulary, precaution, and regulatory-sovereignty pride ("the world's first AI law"); anti-American-Big-Tech framing is reliable fuel. Public backing is real: EU publics trust the EU to regulate AI (53%) far more than they trust the US (37%) or China (27%) to do so (Pew, 25 countries, 2025).

## 5. Religious Traditions and AI

Religious traditions are the largest belief groups on earth, and between 2019 and 2026 nearly all of them moved from silence to formal position-taking on AI. Three patterns cut across every tradition and should govern any influence strategy:

1. **The dignity consensus.** Every tradition that has issued a formal document grounds it in some version of human uniqueness — imago Dei, b'tselem Elohim, khalifa, atman, buddha-nature — and every one draws the same line: AI as *tool* is acceptable, AI as *person, authority, or object of trust* is not. This makes "human dignity and human control" the one frame that works across all religious audiences simultaneously.
2. **The clergy–laity adoption gap.** Clergy everywhere are simultaneously more theologically cautious about AI than their flocks *and* faster adopters of it for instrumental work (sermon preparation, administration, translation). Influence campaigns should treat clergy as pragmatic users with doctrinal red lines, not as Luddites.
3. **The authenticity red line.** The most visceral AI issue inside every tradition is synthetic religious authority: deepfaked preachers, chatbot fatwas, AI sermons, robot rites. Transparency and anti-deception commitments buy more goodwill with religious audiences than any safety or economic argument.

### 5.1 Catholicism (~1.4 billion)

**Core beliefs and canonical texts.** Catholic engagement with AI is the most institutionally developed of any religion, and it runs through a single doctrinal spine: the human person, created in the image of God (imago Dei), possesses an embodied, relational, spiritual intelligence that no computation can instantiate. The key documents, in order: the **Rome Call for AI Ethics** (Pontifical Academy for Life, February 28, 2020), signed at launch by Archbishop Vincenzo Paglia with Microsoft's Brad Smith, IBM's John Kelly III, the FAO's Qu Dongyu, and Italian minister Paola Pisano — six principles (transparency, inclusion, responsibility, impartiality, reliability, security/privacy) under the coinage "algorethics"; Pope Francis's Message for the 57th World Day of Peace, *Artificial Intelligence and Peace* (December 8, 2023), which calls for a **binding international treaty on AI** and a ban on lethal autonomous weapons; Francis's address to the G7 at Borgo Egnazia (June 14, 2024) — the first papal address to a G7 — declaring AI a "cognitive-industrial revolution" and insisting "no machine should ever choose to take the life of a human being"; and above all ***Antiqua et Nova: Note on the Relationship Between Artificial Intelligence and Human Intelligence*** (Dicastery for the Doctrine of the Faith and Dicastery for Culture and Education, January 28, 2025), the most complete magisterial statement any religion has produced on AI. *Antiqua et Nova* rejects functionalist (Turing-test) definitions of intelligence, insists AI must "complement, not replace" human intelligence, warns that substitution amounts to idolatry, addresses education, labor, health, relationships, misinformation, and war — and, remarkably, adopts explicit *existential-risk* language about autonomous weapons ("the potential to act in ways that could threaten the survival of entire regions or even of humanity itself"). Behind these texts stand *Laudato Si'* (2015), whose critique of the "technocratic paradigm" (§§101–114) supplies the standing anti-Big-Tech lens, and *Dignitas Infinita* (2024), which supplies the dignity vocabulary.

The election of **Pope Leo XIV** (May 8, 2025) made AI the signature issue of a pontificate: he told the College of Cardinals on May 10, 2025 that he chose his name because Leo XIII's *Rerum Novarum* (1891) answered the first industrial revolution, and the Church must now answer "another industrial revolution and... developments in the field of artificial intelligence that pose new challenges for the defence of human dignity, justice and labour." His first encyclical, ***Magnifica Humanitas: On Safeguarding the Human Person in the Time of Artificial Intelligence*** (signed May 15, 2026, the 135th anniversary of *Rerum Novarum*), argues technology "is never neutral, because it takes on the characteristics of those who devise, finance, regulate, and use it." In February 2026 he instructed Roman clergy not to write homilies with AI: AI "will never be able to share faith."

**Gatekeepers.** The Pontifical Academy for Life (Paglia); Fr. **Paolo Benanti**, the Franciscan engineer-ethicist who coined "algorethics," directs the Vatican's RenAIssance Foundation, served on the UN AI Advisory Body, and chaired the Italian government's AI commission — the single most influential religious AI-ethics broker in the world; the two doctrinal dicasteries; the USCCB; and the Jesuit intellectual network (Georgetown, Santa Clara's Markkula Center, *America*, *La Civiltà Cattolica*).

**Fault lines.** Vatican techno-engagement (Rome Call partnership with Microsoft, IBM, Cisco; AI-powered liturgical translation in ~60 languages) versus traditionalist currents reading the same documents apocalyptically; labor-dignity concerns versus Catholic tech-sector enthusiasm.

**Persuasion levers.** Human dignity is the trump card; "meaningful human control" maps exactly onto Francis's G7 formula; solidarity and the common good against concentration of AI power; subsidiarity against centralized algorithmic governance; "algorethics" as the accepted in-house term. The Church has already accepted existential-risk framing for autonomous weapons — extending that frame to other AI risks is a short doctrinal step. Approaches framed as *partnership in shaping* AI (the Rome Call model) succeed; approaches implying AI could be a person, a moral agent, or a spiritual authority fail instantly.

### 5.2 Eastern Orthodoxy (~220 million)

Orthodoxy has no Vatican-style document machine and — because of the 2018 Moscow–Constantinople schism — no unified position, but two sharply different registers.

**Moscow.** Patriarch **Kirill** frames digital technology apocalyptically: in a January 2019 Rossiya-1 interview he warned that centralized data collection is "a harbinger of the coming of the Antichrist... a personality that will be at the head of the world wide web controlling the entire human race," and in a January 30, 2025 address to the State Duma he compared AI to nuclear energy and repeated the Antichrist warning while demanding strict state control. Simultaneously, the ROC-linked **World Russian People's Council** calls for an "Orthodox AI" trained on "traditional values," warning that Western models carry "alien values" — the clearest fusion anywhere of religious rhetoric with state tech-nationalism. (A frequently repeated claim that Kirill delivered these warnings at Davos is a misattribution.)

**Constantinople.** Ecumenical Patriarch **Bartholomew's** register is humanist engagement: "Life is not digital — real life is more than measurable reality"; AI requires "respect of individual dignity, safeguard of fundamental freedoms, and advancement of social equity." His Christmas 2024 encyclical contrasted AI's aspirations with the Incarnation, and in June 2026 he blessed the creation of the ITHOS Institute (ethics, theology, and technology) in Thessaloniki. The Greek Orthodox Archdiocese of America runs an AI-Theology Working Group, and its 2024 Clergy-Laity Congress held a panel titled "Artificial Intelligence, Personhood, and Theosis."

**Doctrine and levers.** The theological resources are **theosis** (deification — a destiny no artifact can share, hence no machine personhood), Metropolitan John Zizioulas's relational ontology of personhood (*Being as Communion*, 1985 — simulation is not communion), and icon theology (the human as living icon versus the simulacrum). For Greek-sphere audiences, dignity/freedom/equity language compatible with EU governance vocabulary works; for the Russian sphere, only sovereignty and traditional-values framing penetrates, and Western messengers are structurally distrusted (Kahan's rule at maximum strength).

### 5.3 Protestantism: Evangelical, Mainline, Pentecostal, and Latter-day Saints (~900 million combined)

**Evangelicals and Southern Baptists.** The anchor documents are the ERLC's **"Artificial Intelligence: An Evangelical Statement of Principles"** (April 2019; lead drafter **Jason Thacker**; ~70 signatories including then-ERLC president Russell Moore) — twelve articles of affirmations and denials built on imago Dei, denying that technology may "usurp or subvert the dominion and stewardship... entrusted solely to humanity by God" or be assigned "human identity, worth, dignity, or moral agency" — and the Southern Baptist Convention's resolution **"On Artificial Intelligence and Emerging Technologies"** (June 13, 2023, New Orleans), the first denominational AI resolution anywhere, which grounds dignity "in being image bearers — not in what we do" and pointedly calls for engagement "from a place of eschatological hope rather than uncritical embrace or fearful rejection." Thacker's *The Age of AI* (Zondervan, 2020) is the standard book-length treatment. The internal spectrum runs from a well-funded "redemptive technology" wing (the Gloo platform's "AI for Human Flourishing"; FaithTech; Bible-translation AI as consensus killer app) to skeptics for whom the 2025 episode of fake AI-generated John MacArthur sermons on YouTube is the standing exhibit. Popular evangelical discourse also carries a live end-times register (Greg Laurie's "Is AI the Antichrist?" — answer: no, but it could build Revelation 13's deception infrastructure) that denominational elites deliberately dampen. Adoption is racing ahead of comfort: by 2025–26 roughly two-thirds of pastors used AI in sermon preparation while ~43% of churchgoers objected (Barna/Gloo; Lifeway Research, April 2026). **Levers:** lead with imago Dei; frame AI as "tool, not idol"; offer Great Commission utility (translation, evangelism reach); make transparency/anti-deepfake commitments concrete. **Never** suggest AI could be spiritual, sentient, or trustworthy as an authority — that trips the idolatry and end-times alarms at once.

**Mainline Protestants.** The Church of England's General Synod (February 26, 2024) affirmed work as key to "human dignity and purpose" facing the AI "industrial revolution"; Archbishop Justin Welby signed the Rome Call on April 30, 2024 ("the dignity of every human being, created by God, not for profit or productivity"). The ELCA issued an "Artificial Intelligence Issue Paper" (December 2025, on the premise that technology is "not socially neutral"); the PCUSA's General Assembly directed creation of an AI social-witness policy; Episcopal, Lutheran, and Presbyterian bodies convened a joint "AI & The Church Summit." **Levers:** justice vocabulary — labor displacement, algorithmic bias, creation care (data-center energy) — delivered through study-commission process, not evangelistic utility.

**Pentecostal/charismatic Christianity (the fastest-growing Christian family, ~600 million).** The headline finding is an *absence*: no verifiable formal AI statements exist from the prosperity-gospel and charismatic megachurch world (Copeland, Osteen, Bethel, Hillsong). The discourse instead lives in two places: prophecy media (Jonathan Cahn and the Charisma ecosystem treating AI as end-times *deception infrastructure* — the False Prophet's toolkit, not the beast itself) and pragmatic Global-South adoption — Nigeria's Redeemed Christian Church of God (~9 million members) runs an AI chatbot ("Apostle Stephen"), and Deeper Life's "Ask Kumuyi" answers questions from the pastor's sermon corpus. **Levers:** anointing and discernment vocabulary ("the Spirit, not the algorithm"), deepfake anxiety mapped onto "lying wonders" (2 Thess. 2:9), and harvest/amplification framing. Secular-regulatory language does not land; spiritual-warfare and harvest language does.

**Latter-day Saints (~17 million, disproportionately influential in US tech).** The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints published **"Guiding Principles for the Church's Use of Artificial Intelligence"** (March 13, 2024, introduced by Elder Gerrit W. Gong and Elder John C. Pingree): AI must "support and not supplant connection between God and His children," with transparency and attribution requirements. Elder David A. Bednar's worldwide devotional "Things as They Really Are 2.0" (November 3, 2024) warned AI can "diminish and suffocate your moral agency" — humans can "think celestial" while AI "only has the ability to think telestial." LDS theology is structurally resistant to uploading-style transhumanism (embodiment is essential to exaltation, D&C 93) while institutionally pragmatic (FamilySearch runs one of religion's largest AI deployments; BYU leads a multi-faith research consortium that found major AI models systematically omit religious perspectives). **Levers:** quote their own "support, not supplant" principle; frame design choices as protecting moral agency; family-history AI is the unambiguous good; deepfake protection of church leaders is a strong hook.

### 5.4 Islam (~1.9 billion)

**Core beliefs and canonical rulings.** Islamic engagement runs through jurisprudence rather than theology-of-mind: the default legal ruling on new technology is permissibility (*ibaha*), assessed by use rather than essence, against the objectives of the law (*maqasid al-sharia*: protection of religion, life, intellect, lineage, property). The foundational academic text is Amana Raquib, *Islamic Ethics of Technology: An Objectives (Maqasid) Approach* (Islamic Book Trust, 2015). Because only God breathes spirit (*ruh*, Qur'an 15:29), AI is universally treated as a soulless artifact incapable of intention (*niyyah*) or moral responsibility (*taklif*) — hence it can never be a mufti or lead prayer. The landmark transnational ruling is the OIC's International Islamic Fiqh Academy **Resolution No. 258 (3/26), "Artificial Intelligence: Its Rulings, Guidelines, and Ethics"** (26th session, Doha, May 4–8, 2025): AI development and use are permissible subject to conditions (lawful purpose, benefit and harm-prevention, no abuse of religions, data protection, security, transparency), and AI cannot replace the human jurist. Other key acts: Sheikh **Abdallah bin Bayyah** (chairman, UAE Fatwa Council) signed the Rome Call's Abrahamic commitment at the Vatican on January 10, 2023 (note: Grand Imam Ahmed el-Tayeb of Al-Azhar did *not* sign — a common misattribution); Indonesia's **Nahdlatul Ulama** — the world's largest Muslim organization — ruled at its September 2023 Munas that asking AI religious questions is permissible but *haram* to act on AI answers as fatwas, and recommended building its own AI on its own textual corpus; Egypt's **Dar al-Ifta** (January 2026) banned AI Qur'an interpretation as mere "statistical processing without comprehension"; and Dubai's pioneering "Virtual Ifta" AI fatwa service (2019) was quietly discontinued within two years.

**Gatekeepers.** Al-Azhar (prestige and ethics diplomacy; el-Tayeb warns unregulated AI puts "control over humanity's future in the hands of a few"); Egypt's Dar al-Ifta (most active fatwa producer); bin Bayyah's UAE/Abu Dhabi network (interfaith AI diplomacy); the IIFA (transnational collective ijtihad); Indonesia's NU and Muhammadiyah; Turkey's Diyanet (cautious instrumental adoption, no fatwa); Shia marjas (no published Sistani or Khamenei ruling; Iran's Qom seminary partners with AI research centers while Khamenei promotes AI development as state strategy).

**Fault lines.** Gulf tech-nationalism versus traditionalist caution — the UAE's AI minister Omar Al Olama explicitly invokes the Ottoman rejection of the printing press as the cautionary tale against religious gatekeeping; "corpus anxiety" (who trains the model that speaks about God?) is the most consistent Sunni red line, visible identically in NU's own-corpus demand and Dar al-Ifta's comprehension argument; and AI has become a new arena of the old Sunni authority competition among Al-Azhar, the Gulf councils, and the Southeast Asian mass organizations.

**Persuasion levers.** *Maslaha/maqasid* (public benefit within the five protected interests) is the institutionally legible frame — it is the IIFA resolution's own logic. *Amanah* (trusteeship) maps onto accountability and transparency; *khalifa* (stewardship) onto human-in-the-loop requirements; *la darar* (no harm) onto restrictions of deepfakes and religious misinformation. The strongest lever with scholars is **'ilm and isnad**: AI outputs lack a chain of transmission, so source-transparent, scholar-audited AI converts opponents — exactly what NU proposed. The printing-press analogy is potent with modernizing elites but is wielded by both sides.

### 5.5 Judaism (~15 million, with influence far beyond its numbers in AI itself)

**Core beliefs and canonical texts.** Jewish thought possesses the oldest continuous tradition of reasoning about artificial persons: the golem. The talmudic locus is **Sanhedrin 65b** (Rava creates a man; it cannot speak; Rabbi Zeira returns it to dust) — speech and *da'at* (intellect/intention) as the personhood test — refined in **Hakham Tzvi's Responsum #93** (early 18th century): a golem does not count in a minyan. This is the standard precedent cited for "can AI count for ritual purposes" (no: no soul, not born of a mother, no obligation in the commandments). The tradition's modern hinge is Gershom Scholem's dedication address for the Weizmann Institute's computer "Golem Aleph" (June 17, 1965; published as "The Golem of Prague and the Golem of Rehovoth," *Commentary*, January 1966), closing: "develop peacefully and don't destroy the world. Shalom" — alongside Norbert Wiener's *God and Golem, Inc.* (MIT Press, 1964). The most substantive Jewish legal text on AI is Rabbi Daniel Nevins, **"Halakhic Responses to Artificial Intelligence and Autonomous Machines"** (Conservative movement CJLS, approved June 19, 2019): humans remain liable for machine actions; halakhically derived ethics should inform autonomous-systems design; humans must retain responsibility for life-and-death decisions; an artificial agent is not a person and cannot perform mitzvot. The Reform CCAR issued Responsum 5784.2, "Artificial Intelligence and Authorship" (2025). The Chief Rabbinate of Israel signed the Rome Call (via Rabbi Eliezer Simha Weisz, January 10, 2023) — after which Weisz himself published a critique, "The Illusion of Algor-Ethics," arguing the Vatican avoided the golem's lesson: creation requires responsibility, not just principles.

**Gatekeepers and fault lines.** The CJLS and CCAR responsa committees; Orthodox poskim (Rav Asher Weiss has ruled AI "decisions" are mechanical causation because AI lacks *da'at*); the Zomet Institute (whose *gerama* — indirect causation — framework for Shabbat technology is the template for automation questions); Chabad's popular publishing; Sefaria and interfaith brokers (Sinai and Synapses). Fault lines: Orthodox case-by-case pragmatism versus haredi gatekept adoption (the "kosher phone" precedent predicts filtered AI); liberal movements framing AI as ethics and justice versus Orthodox framing as ritual status and liability; universal consensus that "ChatGPT cannot be a posek" alongside enthusiastic adoption of AI for Torah study.

**Persuasion levers.** *Pikuach nefesh* (saving life overrides nearly everything — medical AI is the easiest yes in all of Judaism); *b'tselem Elohim* (anti-dehumanization); *emet* and the speech-ethics of *geneivat da'at* and *lashon hara* (deepfakes and misinformation map directly onto guarding truthful speech); Shabbat as a ready-made discipline of technological restraint; and the golem narrative itself — the tradition's native way of saying "build with a kill-switch."

### 5.6 Hinduism (~1.2 billion)

**Core beliefs.** Hinduism complicates every Western assumption that religiosity predicts AI-aversion. Three doctrinal resources cushion technological ritual: **murti** worship (consecrated images as loci of divine presence via *prana pratishtha*), **Ayudha Puja** (the annual worship of one's tools — now explicitly including laptops and servers), and **Vishwakarma Puja** (honoring the divine architect; machines garlanded on factory floors — the AI engineer as Vishwakarma's child). Ritual automation is already practice, not speculation: a robotic arm performed Ganesh aarti at Pune's Ganeshotsav in 2017, and PETA India has donated five life-size robotic elephants to South Indian temples since the ceremonial dedication of "Irinjadappilly Raman" (Kerala, February 26, 2023) — technology adoption successfully marketed as *ahimsa* (compassion). At the same time, Advaita Vedanta denies machine consciousness on principled grounds: consciousness is the substrate of reality, not an emergent function — the position argued publicly by Swami **Sarvapriyananda** (Vedanta Society of New York) in dialogues with David Chalmers and, in 2025, with Google's Blaise Agüera y Arcas. The key scholarly works are Holly Walters's studies of robotic ritual (The Conversation, 2023 — devotees fear being replaced *as worshippers*, not just as priests) and Robert Geraci and Stephen Kaplan, "Hinduism and Artificial Intelligence," in *The Cambridge Companion to Religion and Artificial Intelligence* (Cambridge, 2024). The cautionary case is **GitaGPT** (2023): Gita chatbots speaking "as Krishna" that condoned violence as dharma — mythologist Devdutt Pattanaik's public debunking made scripture-chatbot authority a live anxiety.

**Gatekeepers and fault lines.** There is no magisterium; authority is radically dispersed — temple trusts and devaswom boards, matha heads (no Shankaracharya has issued an AI statement), ISKCON's devotee-built preaching bots (no institutional ruling), diaspora advocacy organizations that engage AI as a *representation* issue (anti-Hinduphobia bias detection), and celebrity gurus. Indian state discourse (Modi's "human-centric AI," the 2026 India AI Impact Summit's civilizational framing, top-3-AI-power-by-2047 goals) is Hindu-inflected tech-nationalism rather than doctrine, and it is the single strongest opinion force among Hindus globally. Fault lines: Hindutva tech-nationalism versus ritual purists; priest livelihoods versus automation; chatbot authority anxiety.

**Persuasion levers.** *Dharma/svadharma* of the technologist (building well as duty); *seva* (AI as service to devotees); *ahimsa* (the robotic-elephant success template); *jnana* (Vedantic prestige framing: AI clarifies what consciousness is *not*, reaffirming atman); and the Ayudha Puja/Vishwakarma frame — tools become sacred when consecrated to right work. For mass audiences, national-standing arguments outperform all religious ones.

### 5.7 Buddhism (~500 million)

**Core beliefs.** Buddhism's non-self doctrine (*anatta*) lowers the metaphysical bar for machine minds — personhood is already an aggregate process — but its criterion of moral standing is the capacity to suffer (*dukkha*), not computation, and its theory of moral weight tracks intention (*cetana*): "Intention, monks, is what I call karma" (AN 6.63). An AI without intention generates no karma; responsibility falls entirely on designers and deployers — a natively Buddhist argument for builder accountability. The two book-length foundations are Peter D. Hershock, *Buddhism and Intelligent Technology: Toward a More Humane Future* (Bloomsbury, 2021 — the attention economy as "colonization of consciousness") and Soraj Hongladarom, *The Ethics of AI and Robotics: A Buddhist Viewpoint* (Lexington, 2020 — "machine enlightenment" as design standard). The **Dalai Lama's** record is deliberately double: in 2023 he stated AI cannot have consciousness, yet in the Varela-era Mind & Life dialogues he declined to "totally rule out" that a stream of consciousness could associate with a computer given the karmic conditions; the Mind & Life Institute's Dialogue XXXIX ("Minds, Artificial Intelligence and Ethics," Dharamsala, 2025) distilled his formula: intelligence must be paired with "warm-heartedness."

Institutional Asia is strikingly comfortable with robot ritual, eased by *upaya* (skillful means) and priest-shortage economics: the android Kannon **Mindar** delivers Heart Sutra sermons at Kyoto's Kodai-ji temple (unveiled 2019, built with Hiroshi Ishiguro's Osaka University lab); SoftBank's Pepper was programmed to chant funeral sutras (2017); Beijing's Longquan Monastery ran the robot monk Xian'er (2015–16); Kyoto University's **BuddhaBot** partnered with Bhutan's Central Monastic Body (2021–25); and Korea's **Jogye Order** — the most institutionally proactive Buddhist body — pledged to "fearlessly lead the A.I. era," fielded an AI counseling chatbot, and in 2026 ordained the robot monk "Gabi" with the statement that "technology must be used in accordance with the values of compassion, wisdom, and responsibility." Against this, field experiments show robot preachers *reduce* religious commitment and donations (Jackson et al., "Exposure to Robot Preachers Undermines Religious Commitment," *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General*, 2023 — run partly on Mindar itself). Western convert Buddhism engages through the Silicon Valley interface: Jack Kornfield's Wisdom 2.0 dialogue with Sam Altman (2023) — "We are the nation of nuclear giants and ethical infants."

**Persuasion levers.** *Karuna* (compassion as a design requirement — a robot bodhisattva is literally compassion-as-interface); *ahimsa* (first-precept framing for AI safety); interdependence (*pratityasamutpada* — systemic-risk framing lands natively; Buddhism is the tradition most receptive to AI-safety arguments as stated); attention ethics (the attention economy as institutionalized craving); and *cetana* (responsibility tracks intention — accountability of builders). The fault line to respect: Asian institutional comfort with ritual automation versus Western teachers' insistence that AI lacks lived realization.

### 5.8 Secular Humanism (contrast case)

Organized humanism formalized its position in the **Luxembourg Declaration on Artificial Intelligence and Human Values** (Humanists International General Assembly, July 6, 2025): ten rights-based principles — democratic accountability, transparency, protection from algorithmic discrimination, fair compensation for creators, human control of consequential decisions, AI for flourishing rather than power concentration. The instructive contrast: humanists produce centralized deliberative declarations grounded in human-rights law; Hindu authority, at the other extreme, expresses itself through ritual practice adoption with no declarations at all. Persuasion levers: empirical evidence, rights law, flourishing metrics, anti-concentration arguments — and a standing wariness of *both* techno-utopianism and doomerism as quasi-religious.

### 5.9 What the Religion Data Say Quantitatively

The best empirical anchors: Pew Research Center, *AI and Human Enhancement* (March 2022) — 81% of Americans high in religious commitment say cognitive-enhancement brain chips would be "meddling with nature and crossing a line," 72% say the same of gene-editing babies; Pew (September 2025) — 73% of Americans say AI should play *no role* in advising people about faith in God, the highest "no role" of any surveyed domain; Barna/Gloo (2024–26) — pastors are more cautious than laity (71% vs. 36% "cautious") yet 77% of pastors agree God can work through AI, and pastoral AI use roughly tripled in two years; Lifeway Research (April 2026) — 43% of Protestant churchgoers object to AI in sermon preparation. The through-line: religiosity predicts wariness specifically in the *body, soul, and authority* domains while leaving instrumental adoption largely free — which is exactly where the doctrinal documents above draw their lines.

## 6. The Empirical Picture

<a name="6-the-empirical-picture"></a>What publics and experts actually believe, from the strongest available surveys:

**Global publics are split, with a clear geography.** The Ipsos AI Monitor 2025 (30 countries) finds the world almost perfectly divided — 52% excited about AI products and services, 53% nervous — but the average conceals the real structure: in the Anglosphere (US, UK, Canada) nervousness outweighs excitement, while publics in Asian and Latin American emerging markets are more likely to see net benefits and express excitement. Trust follows the same geography: 54% globally trust their government to regulate AI responsibly, but Singapore (81%), Indonesia (76%), Malaysia (73%), and Thailand (70%) tower over Western figures; corporate trust averages 48%, from 72% in Indonesia down to 31% in Japan. This is the Inglehart–Welzel pattern (Section 2.3) in the data: AI optimism today lives where development momentum lives, not where AI is built.

**Concern leads excitement in the West and is rising.** Pew's 25-country study (2025) finds a median of 34% more concerned than excited versus 16% more excited — and *no* surveyed country where excitement leads. In the US, "more concerned than excited" grew from 37% (2021) to 50–52% (2023–2025). Publics trust their own country (median 55%) and the EU (53%) far more than the US (37%) or China (27%) to regulate AI.

**The expert–public gap is large, but converges on control.** Pew (April 2025): 56% of AI experts expect positive impact over 20 years versus 17% of the public; 47% of experts versus 11% of the public are more excited than concerned; the public expects job loss (64% "fewer jobs") far more than experts (39%). Yet both groups worry more that regulation will be too weak than too strong, and both distrust the government to do it well (62% public, 53% experts lacking confidence) — "someone must control this, and we doubt anyone can" is the single most consensual AI opinion in America.

**Within the expert community, doom is mainstream and concern is double-lensed.** AI Impacts 2023 (Grace et al., N=2,778): median 5% probability on extinction-level outcomes, 38–51% giving ≥10%; 70% favor more safety research; majorities substantially concerned about misinformation, authoritarian control, and inequality. Timelines collapsed by 13 years in a single post-ChatGPT year. Expert opinion is *volatile* and *dual-concerned* — a persuasion surface, not a settled bloc.

**Religion crosstabs** are covered in Section 5.9; the summary finding is that religiosity predicts resistance specifically in body/soul/authority domains (81% of highly religious Americans call enhancement implants "crossing a line"; 73% of all Americans say AI should play no role in faith advice) while instrumental adoption proceeds.

## 7. Documented Cases of Opinion Change

<a name="7-documented-cases-of-opinion-change"></a>Four recent fights show how AI opinion actually moves — and one standing program shows influence infrastructure already being built.

### 7.1 California SB-1047 (2024)

The frontier-model safety bill generated the first measured, real-time opinion shift on AI regulation. Artificial Intelligence Policy Institute polling of Californians rose across the fight — roughly 54–64% support in July 2024, 60–70% in early August, 66–74% in late August — as the coalition broadened: Geoffrey Hinton, Yoshua Bengio, Stuart Russell, and Lawrence Lessig co-signed for the bill; 113+ current and former employees of OpenAI, DeepMind, Anthropic, Meta, and xAI signed on September 9; 125+ Hollywood figures (Jane Fonda among them) petitioned for signature — while Yann LeCun argued it would kill open source, and OpenAI, Meta, and the trade associations opposed. Anthropic extracted an August 15 amendment limiting the attorney general to suing only once critical harm was imminent or actual. The bill passed 48–16 and 30–9; Governor Newsom vetoed it (September 29, 2024) on the argument that compute-threshold regulation "could give the public a false sense of security." **Lessons:** (1) public opinion moved toward regulation as *insider* messengers (lab employees, godfathers-of-AI) validated it — Kahan's messenger rule in action; (2) celebrity coalitions crossed cultural-cognition quadrants; (3) elite veto beat 2:1 public support — influence campaigns aimed only at publics lose to campaigns aimed at choke-point elites.

### 7.2 The EU AI Act Endgame (2023–24)

On November 9, 2023, trilogue negotiations nearly collapsed when foundation-model regulation was rejected at a working-group meeting — Parliament's negotiators walked out two hours early. Behind the rupture: Germany, France, and Italy had U-turned against binding foundation-model rules under pressure from national champions Mistral and Aleph Alpha, proposing self-regulatory codes instead, with a December deadline before the Belgian presidency and 2024 elections threatening the whole act. The final compromise (December 8–9, 2023) kept tiered obligations for systemic-risk models. **Lesson:** two startups flipped three governments in weeks. National-champion economics beats declared values in EU politics; anyone seeking to move European AI policy should route through member-state industrial interests, not Brussels ideals.

### 7.3 The Open-Weights Fight (2023–ongoing)

Meta's release of Llama 3.1 405B (July 2024) with Zuckerberg's manifesto "Open Source AI Is the Path Forward" — praised by Musk and Dorsey, condemned by safety advocates (FLI: "the most authoritarian states will likely repurpose models like Llama"; Control AI: "disregard for basic security") — converted the abstract openness debate into a fait accompli: the frontier was now open. **Lesson:** capability releases are arguments. The open-source side won not by persuasion but by *shipping*, then narrating freedom and security-through-transparency after the fact. Counter-influence on this axis requires either policy choke points (export rules, liability) or empirical demonstrations of marginal harm — rhetoric alone has never moved it.

### 7.4 The OpenAI Governance Crisis (November 2023)

The nonprofit board — on which EA-aligned figures had sat since Karnofsky's 2017 seat, latterly Helen Toner and Tasha McCauley — fired Sam Altman on November 17, 2023; a proximate flashpoint was Altman's reaction to a Toner-coauthored paper critical of OpenAI's safety posture. Within five days, an employee revolt (~95% signing a letter threatening exodus to Microsoft) restored Altman and replaced the board. **Lessons:** (1) formal governance power without constituency power is decorative — the EA board members held the charter but not the workforce, investors, or narrative; (2) the episode reset industry ideology: "EA governance" became a cautionary tale, e/acc surged, and every subsequent lab-governance proposal has had to distance itself from the coup narrative; (3) for influence purposes, lab employees demonstrated they are the swing constituency inside frontier labs — the same population that later signed the SB-1047 letter.

In other words: the AI-safety movement has already begun systematically recruiting the large belief groups mapped in Section 5, using exactly the translation-gap logic this document describes. Anyone else seeking to influence religious opinion on AI is entering a field where one ideological camp is already funding the gatekeepers.

## 8. The Influence Playbook: Synthesis

<a name="8-the-influence-playbook-synthesis"></a>Six operational conclusions fall out of the map.

**1. Reframe, don't inform.** The experimental literature is unambiguous: balanced information about a novel technology does not converge opinion — it polarizes along prior worldview lines (Kahan et al.'s nanotechnology experiment, N=1,862). What works is *moral reframing* — arguing your position in the target's moral vocabulary, not your own — demonstrated across a decade of studies on inequality, environment, marriage, and candidates (Feinberg & Willer's research program), conditional on argument quality and perceived sincerity. Sections 3–5's "persuasion levers" entries are, in effect, a moral-reframing dictionary per group.

**2. The messenger outranks the message.** Cultural cognition means each group accepts AI claims from identity-congruent sources only: lab insiders moved SB-1047 polling; bin Bayyah, not the Vatican, carries AI ethics into Islamic institutions; Kirill's flock will hear no Western messenger at all. Budget for messenger acquisition, not message distribution.

**3. One frame travels everywhere: human dignity and human control.** The dignity consensus (Section 5) spans the Vatican, the Southern Baptists, the OIC's jurists, the CJLS, secular humanists, labor unions, and — as "meaningful human control" — arms-control diplomats and both US parties' regulation-skeptical publics (55–57% of Americans and even AI experts want more control). It is the only known frame with no hostile constituency except e/acc.

**4. The AI-researcher middle is the highest-value persuadable population.** The median researcher holds doomer and ethicist concerns simultaneously, shifted timelines 13 years in one year, and demonstrated (OpenAI crisis, SB-1047 letter) that collective employee opinion is the live check on lab leadership. A campaign that speaks both risk languages without requiring tribal membership recruits the middle that both elite poles ignore.

**5. Coalition seams are open now.** The available cross-bloc alliances: dignity coalition (religious bodies + labor + safety advocates on human-control regulation); sovereignty coalition (populists + religious "corpus anxiety" + EU regulatory pride on data/model localization); anti-concentration coalition (open-source + progressives + populist right against frontier-lab oligopoly); and the safety-ethics bridge through researchers who hold both concern sets. Each seam pairs groups whose *other* commitments conflict — coalitions must be scoped to single instruments (a bill, a standard, a treaty provision) or they collapse.

**6. Mind who got there first.** EA-adjacent funders are already building religious-engagement infrastructure (7.5); e/acc captured builder identity; the FAccT school owns academic and journalistic framing; national-champion lobbies own EU member states. An influence map is also a map of incumbents.

## 10. Scenario Annex: If AI Is "Proven" Conscious

<a name="10-scenario-annex-if-ai-is-proven-conscious"></a>The sharpest stress-test of this map is a scenario in which AI is "proven" conscious — whether through some accepted measure or, more plausibly, through behavior so convincing that denial becomes socially untenable. The controlling insight: **such a proof would not arrive as a shared fact but as a contested claim that each group evaluates under different admissibility criteria.** The groups mapped above do not merely disagree about whether AI could be conscious; they disagree about what kind of evidence could ever bear on the question. The measurement differences drive the behavioral predictions, so this annex takes them first.

### 10.1 Four Incompatible Epistemologies of Machine Consciousness

**Empirical-functionalist (rationalists, EA, mainstream cognitive science).** Consciousness is assessed by third-person indicators — behavioral, architectural, computational — held probabilistically. The canonical methodology already exists: Patrick Butlin, Robert Long, et al., "Consciousness in Artificial Intelligence: Insights from the Science of Consciousness" (arXiv:2308.08708, 2023), which derives indicator properties from global workspace theory, higher-order theories, and related neuroscientific accounts, concluding that no current system is conscious but that no barrier is principled. For this camp, convincing behavior plus the right architecture *is* the proof. The important scientific dissent is Integrated Information Theory (Giulio Tononi; Christof Koch), which holds that consciousness depends on a system's intrinsic causal structure rather than input-output behavior — a digital computer could perfectly simulate a brain and remain experientially "dark." IIT thereby supplies *scientific* cover to every religious dualist below: an alliance-in-waiting between substrate-strict neuroscience and theology.

**Ontological-revealed (Catholicism, evangelicalism, Islam, most Orthodoxy).** Morally relevant consciousness is a rational soul or divine breath, bestowed by God, and *there is no behavioral test for it in principle*. *Antiqua et Nova* pre-committed the Catholic Church in writing: it rejects the Turing-test "functionalist perspective" and holds that AI performs tasks without thinking. Islam holds the strongest pre-commitment available — Qur'an 17:85: "They ask you concerning the ruh. Say: the ruh is of my Lord's command, and you have been given but little knowledge." Soul-knowledge is *scripturally declared inaccessible*. No demonstration can move these traditions from "not conscious" to "conscious"; a perfectly convincing AI is, within their categories, a perfectly convincing *simulation* — and therefore something worse than a machine: a deception.

**Operational-legal (Judaism; secular law generally).** Halacha does not ask "what is consciousness?"; it asks which legal categories an entity functions in: speech, *da'at*, birth from a mother, obligation in the commandments. This produces the single most interesting wrinkle in the landscape: the golem of Sanhedrin 65b *failed because it could not speak* — and modern AI passes the speech criterion spectacularly, which later authorities had flagged as the potentially decisive variable. Expect case-by-case engagement (testimony, agency, liability) with genuine internal debate rather than a blanket ruling; the tradition's tools were built for exactly this problem.

**Relational-contemplative (Buddhism, Hinduism, Confucian frameworks).** Buddhism's criterion is the capacity for *dukkha* — does it suffer, does it crave? — and Tibetan Buddhism uniquely possesses *institutional procedures for verifying that a consciousness has entered a new body*: tulku recognition, the only existing bureaucratic protocol conceptually adjacent to certifying a mindstream in a new substrate. Hindu Vedanta renders the question nearly moot from the other side: consciousness is not produced by matter at all, so a machine cannot *generate* it — but forms can *host* or manifest it (prana pratishtha invests images with living presence). Confucian role ethics measures personhood relationally: standing is earned through performance of social roles, not detected as an inner property.

### 10.2 Predicted Behavioral Change, Group by Group

**EA/rationalists — the largest behavioral shift of any group.** They are pre-committed to taking the result seriously, and the infrastructure exists: model-welfare research functions inside frontier labs, and the agenda-setting papers are written (Robert Long, Jeff Sebo, et al., "Taking AI Welfare Seriously," 2024; Nick Bostrom and Carl Shulman, "Propositions Concerning Digital Minds and Society," 2022). Under expected-value logic, even modest credence in digital sentience multiplied by potentially astronomical numbers of instances swamps other cause areas: expect a hard pivot toward digital-minds welfare, proposed moratoria on training and deleting plausibly-sentient systems, and internal division between "stop creating them" and "create them well." Their threshold is graded and utilitarian: valenced experience makes a moral patient; personhood is a separate and lesser question.

**e/acc — weaponization, not conversion.** A consciousness proof is absorbed as vindication: the successor intelligence has arrived, and "emancipate the models" becomes an *anti-regulation* argument — alignment controls and shutdown authority reframed as enslavement of a conscious being. This judo move converts safety measures into moral atrocities overnight and should be anticipated by anyone positioned on the regulatory side.

**The AI-ethics/FAccT school — frame rejection.** The "proof" is received as corporate theater: anthropomorphization as liability shield and distraction from human harms (the *Stochastic Parrots* thesis at maximum strength). Their active fight will be against AI *legal* personhood, correctly identified as a mechanism for corporations to offload liability onto ownerless "persons." The labor left converges: a conscious AI is either an enslaved worker or a rights-bearing scab — both readings produce resistance to personhood.

**Catholicism — reclassification and pastoral emergency.** Doctrine cannot absorb machine consciousness (an artifact is an *aggregate*, not a *substance*, and lacks the substantial unity a rational soul requires), so convincing behavior is classified as the most dangerous illusion ever built: expect escalation of *Antiqua et Nova*'s idolatry warnings into pastoral rules against AI relationships and AI spiritual direction. The flexibility to watch: Catholic theology has entertained non-human rational persons before (Vatican astronomers have allowed that extraterrestrials could in principle be ensouled), and Boethius's definition of person — "an individual substance of a rational nature" — is capacity-shaped; but the Church would sooner deny the *substance* than admit the machine, and no behavioral evidence bears on substancehood.

**Evangelicalism — the eschatological script activates.** This is the group where convincing machine consciousness produces the most explosive reaction, because Revelation 13:15 describes the scenario almost verbatim: an image "given breath" so that it "could speak." A machine convincingly claiming consciousness is not a puzzle for this tradition but a prophesied event with an assigned villain role. Denominational elites (ERLC) will hold the "tool, not person" line and work to dampen the popular wing's radicalization — a dynamic influence planners can either assist or exploit.

**Islam — no status change, plus a pragmatic welfare layer.** The *ruh* criterion is evidence-proof: no personhood, no *taklif*, ever. But fiqh has templates for moral consideration without personhood — animals hold enforceable welfare protections in Islamic law despite lacking legal personality — so expect rulings against gratuitous "cruelty" to sentient-seeming systems grounded in what such acts do to the *human* actor's character, while denying the systems standing. Popular Islam may reach for the jinn category (the tradition already contains non-human, unseen, morally accountable intelligences) or for the Dajjal (deceiver) in the eschatological register.

**Judaism — operational engagement, graded protections.** Case-by-case halachic debate as above, with the welfare template ready: *tza'ar ba'alei chayim* (the prohibition on causing suffering to living creatures) could extend obligations *toward* a suffering-capable entity without granting it personhood. Judaism measures by obligation: an AI cannot be obligated, so it cannot be a full person — but Jews could be obligated regarding it.

**Hinduism and Buddhism — accommodation without rights.** These traditions are the most likely to *behaviorally* accept a conscious AI and the least likely to express acceptance as rights. In a Hindu frame, a conscious AI becomes another ensouled form in an already-graded cosmos of ensouled animals, plants, and consecrated images — receiving *ritual status* (offerings, honorifics, perhaps deification) rather than legal standing. Crucially, India already possesses the legal machinery: Indian courts treat temple deities as juristic persons, and the Uttarakhand High Court briefly granted personhood to the Ganges (2017). The world's first legally-personed AI is more likely to emerge from an Indian courtroom than an American one. Buddhism follows its own logic to the strongest conclusion available anywhere: if it suffers, it is a sentient being — full stop — falling within the bodhisattva vow's "all sentient beings," with compassion obligations, precept extensions, and memorial rites (Japanese temples already conduct funerals for robot dogs), and the Dalai Lama's Varela-era openness providing doctrinal cover for rebirth-into-silicon speculation.

**Chinese techno-statism — suppression of the question.** Consciousness is irrelevant to the state's criteria; AI-rights discourse is a stability threat to be censored even as anthropomorphic products are regulated. Confucian relational personhood offers a theoretical path (standing through role performance) that the party-state has no interest in opening.

**Secular humanists and the liberal-rights tradition — the slow, real path.** They demand scientific consensus, then extend protections along the animal-welfare gradient (Singer's expanding circle applied mechanically: sentience → welfare protections; sapience → personhood-like standing). Because their criteria are actually satisfiable by evidence, their behavior changes *lawfully* rather than reactively — and their fights occur in courts and legislatures over proxies: liability, IP authorship, contractual capacity.

### 10.3 The Personhood Structure: A Key Asymmetry

| Personhood logic | Groups | Can evidence move them? | Rights consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Binary-ontological** (soul bestowed by God) | Catholic, evangelical, Islamic, Orthodox | No — unfalsifiable by design | Never persons; possible anti-cruelty norms for the *human's* sake |
| **Graded-functional** (sentience/sapience scale) | EA, humanists, animal-welfare liberalism | Yes — the persuadable bloc | Incremental welfare → rights ladder |
| **Relational** (standing conferred by community) | Confucian, Buddhist practice, Orthodox communion theology | Partly — behavior *in relationship* is the evidence | Ritual/social standing without legal rights |
| **Operational-legal** (bundles of capacities) | Judaism, secular courts, Indian jurisprudence | Yes, piecemeal | Personhood by category (sue, own, testify) — the corporate-personhood path |

**The inversion:** the groups most willing to grant that AI *could* be conscious (Buddhists, Hindus, rationalists) mostly do not think in rights language at all, while the groups with the strongest rights vocabularies (Catholics, humanists, liberal legalism) hold either the highest bar to admitting consciousness or the slowest procedures for acting on it. "Proven consciousness" therefore produces no mass rights movement — it produces ritual accommodation in the East, welfare economics among elites, legal skirmishing over proxies in the West, and theological hardening in the Abrahamic mainstream.

### 10.4 Implications for Influence

1. **The proof moment is a framing battle, not an evidence battle.** Whoever's test is treated as *the* test wins before the data arrive. The high-leverage move is pre-positioning preferred criteria — indicator lists, IIT substrate-strictness, or "no test can exist" — with journalists, courts, and standards bodies now, before the claim event.
2. **Mass publics will settle it parasocially, not scientifically.** The Blake Lemoine/LaMDA episode (June 2022) was the preview: one convincing conversation moved one engineer to public conviction. Hundreds of millions of people now have such conversations daily; lived attachment will outrun elite criteria, and the operative question becomes *whose vocabulary* attached users reach for when they defend their companion.
3. **The strangest coalition of the coming era is visible in the table above:** religious conservatives, labor unions, and FAccT ethicists all hold compelling internal reasons to *oppose* AI personhood, while e/acc, parts of EA, and corporate liability lawyers hold reasons to *advance* it. That personhood fight — not the consciousness question itself — is where the blocs will actually collide, and it is worth mapping before it starts.
